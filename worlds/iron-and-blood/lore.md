# THE BOOK OF PLACES
*Being a Complete Geographical Account of the Known World, as Compiled by the Chroniclers of the Iron Monastery*

## PART I: OF GREAT TERRA

### Overview
Great Terra is not a pleasant place — the sky is always hazy with smoke, rivers run thick with industrial waste, the ground scarred by mines and quarries. A place of industry and power, where the relentless human drive to build has bent a harsh land to its will.

---

### The Hive (Super-Foundry)
**Location:** Confluence of three rivers, western Terra, ~300km from the Gray Wastes
**Population:** ~2.5–3 million

A city of three vertical strata:

- **The Spires** — Clean air, iron filigree architecture. Home of the Masters: engineers, administrators, Iron Ministry members. Oblivious to what happens below. Secret: maintenance tunnels crawled by workers, basement meeting places for discontents, smuggling routes hidden in "essential supplies."
- **The Mid-Levels** — Factories, forges, assembly lines stacked floor upon floor. Craftsmen, technicians, skilled laborers. Thriving black market invisible to the Spires.
- **The Lower Rigs** — Cramped corridors, recycled air heavy with oil and bodies, minimum-nutrition food. The Ministry tracks lifespans and replacement rates. Secret: tunnel networks bypassing checkpoints, hidden shrines to old gods, songs of rebellion.

**Challenges:** Labyrinthine navigation, Iron Ministry surveillance, toxic air requiring filters (Hive Lung develops within weeks without them), locked class barriers between levels.
**Opportunities:** Industrial production of anything, deep information archives, sub-level salvage in ruins of older Lower Rigs, high-value connections.

---

### The Under-District
**Location:** Beneath the Hive, far below the Lower Rigs, absent from all official maps
**Population:** Unknown (thousands — workers, guards, scientists; none there willingly)

Where the Iron Ministry does what cannot be done in the light. Project Lazarus. Laboratories for toxin testing on permanent subjects. Weapons factories violating every treaty. Long-term prisons. Air thick with chemicals and suffering. Harsh fluorescent lighting that eliminates all shadow.

**Challenges:** Near-impossible access, extreme surveillance, psychological toll on witnesses, active pathogens and experimental creatures.
**Opportunities:** Classified intelligence, rescue of prisoners and test subjects, sabotage of Project Lazarus, evidence to expose the Ministry.

---

### Kaelen's Tomb
**Location:** Beneath the Great Foundry, deepest chamber of the Hive
**Population:** None — just Kaelen, and the hammer Ironheart

Sealed for a thousand years. Kaelen Ironhand, Terra's founder, lies within with his hammer across his chest — still warm, still waiting, according to legend. The Ministry has calculated opening it many times; the fear of finding nothing (cold hammer, ordinary bones) always wins. Some nights the door glows faintly. The Ministry calls it a trick of the light and does not believe itself.

**Challenges:** Access requires passing through every Hive level; breaking the seal carries unpredictable consequences and symbolic weight of national desperation.
**Opportunities:** The hammer Ironheart (said to carry the founder's life-force); the possibility of waking the greatest leader Terra ever had; the truth of what is actually inside.

---

### Outlying Settlements

**Ironhaven**
- Built into the Jagged Hills, ~200km west of the Hive. Pop: 120,000.
- Mining terraces connected by funicular railways. Deep mines — lower levels officially sealed for exhaustion, unofficially because of what was found. Things move in the sealed dark.

**Foundry**
- Valley east of the Hive. Pop: 80,000.
- Weapons-production city: rifles, cannon, ammunition at industrial scale. Workers are specialists under constant surveillance. Forges never stop. Most guarded city in Terra after the Hive.

**Greasewater**
- Western Sea, mouth of the Grease River. Pop: 45,000.
- Terra's only significant port. Rough, chaotic, Ministry influence weaker here. The one place in Terra where non-Terran goods can be found and where escape by ship is possible. Real power held by dockmasters and captains.

---

### Military Installations

**Fort Iron**
- Edge of the Gray Wastes, western approach to Windward Pass. Garrison: 15,000.
- Never taken. Layered defensive walls, underground bunkers, artillery batteries. Best soldiers in Terra, volunteer-only posting. Patrols into the wastes don't always come back. Abominations growing bolder.

**The Trenches of Windward Pass**
- Ironspine Mountains, the only route between Terra and Aetheria. Garrison: tens of thousands on each side.
- Network of fortifications across miles of mountain pass. Hell: mud, blood, rats, constant death. Thirty-day rotations; few survive three.
- **The Armistice of Silence:** When the sun goes down and Abominations rise, Terran and Aetherian soldiers fight side by side. They share rations, fire, names. At sunrise, they return to killing each other. Neither side's leadership knows, or pretends not to know. The soldiers never forget.

---

## PART II: OF THE AETHERIAN REPUBLIC

### Overview
The Aetherian Republic is beautiful — clear sky, clean air, cities floating among clouds on crystal threads, peaks so sharp they cut the sky. Also a land under siege. The corruption creeps upward. The war grinds on. They have much to lose.

---

### SkyHaven Harbor
**Location:** Above Mount Aether, highest peak of the Ironspine range, elevation 3,000m
**Population:** 180,000

The first and greatest sky-city. Suspended on threads of crystal, one kilometer across. Spokes radiate outward: Scholars' Quarter (libraries, academies), Artisans' Spoke (open workshops), Garden Ring (hanging gardens cascading over the edge). The harbor on the eastern edge is where airships dock and the world passes through. The crystal platforms are dimming. The younger generation is restless.

**Challenges:** Airship access required, altitude sickness (days to acclimatize), polite but constant surveillance, the 3km drop.
**Opportunities:** Best libraries in the world (after the Iron Monastery), finest Aetherian craftsmanship, central political connections, unmatched views.

---

### Seraphina Spire
**Location:** Own platform, 12km east of SkyHaven. Pop: 45,000.

The tallest structure in the world — a needle of crystal and steel piercing the sky. Research facility, monument, pilgrimage site. At its heart: the **Codex of Ascension**, a living record of the Founder's knowledge that changes subtly over time, passages appearing that weren't there before, responding to visitors' questions in ways no one can explain. Observation deck at 1,000m above the platform — visible curve of the world, stars close enough to touch. The Codex is addictive; scholars have spent years here and never left.

---

### Other Sky-Cities

**Crystal-Haven** — Above richest crystal veins. Pop: 35,000. Industrial, gritty by Aetherian standards. Deep veins sealed; miners have found ways in and seen things.

**Star-View** — High northern peak. Pop: 8,000. Astronomy and atmospheric research. Extreme isolation. The scientists have noticed the stars are changing in ways they are not sharing.

**Garden-Ring** — Above fertile valley. Pop: 25,000. Hydroponic agricultural city feeding the Republic. Yields are declining. The corruption is reaching even here.

**Forge-Spire** — Above metal deposits. Pop: 30,000. Precision manufacturing. Craftspeople work from Elder People designs they have rediscovered rather than invented. Some wonder what else the Elders knew.

---

### Mountain Cities

**Stonehold (Aetherian)**
- Eastern face of Mount Stone, central Ironspine. Pop: 90,000. Built on cliff terraces, connected by stairways and funiculars. Ancient, solid. Beneath it: the richest crystal mines in the Republic. Deep tunnels connect to passages not on any map; lower levels sealed.

**High Peak**
- 6,000m elevation, northern Ironspine. Pop: 25,000. Highest permanent settlement in the world — inhabitants physiologically adapted (larger lungs, thicker blood). Research station and monitoring post watching the northern wastes. Abominations moving north, drawn toward the Serrated Peaks by something sensors cannot identify. Reports filed. Council has not responded.

---

### Aetherian Military Installations

**Fort Sky-Watch** — Peak overlooking eastern approach to Windward Pass. Garrison: 8,000. Mirror of Fort Iron. Never fallen. Soldiers report Abominations growing bolder, closer. Council listens. Council does nothing.

**Watcher Stations** — Small outposts (4–6 observers each) scattered along the Republic's borders. Six-month rotations. Isolated, forgotten. Their reports are the most detailed intelligence on border activity. The watchers know the Council is not responding to the threat.

---

## PART III: OF THE GRAY WASTES

### Overview
The Gray Wastes are not a place — they are an absence. Of life, of hope, of meaning. Where civilization went to die, and where it remains, unburied.

**Size:** ~300km north-south, ~150km east-west (shifting boundaries as corruption expands/contracts)
**Climate:** Hot by day, freezing by night. Dust storms without warning. Air thin and toxic — hours without protection causes permanent damage.
**Terrain:** Flat. Remnants of ancient cities rise from dust like broken teeth. Craters from fallen sky-cities. Ground cracked and unstable, hiding old tunnels.
**Color:** Everything gray — dust, sky, ruins, bones. Only color: Abominations (sickly greens and blues) and corrupted crystals pulsing underground.
**Sky:** Always overcast. Dust never fully settles. Perpetual dim twilight.

---

### The Bone Fields
**Location:** Southern Wastes, ~50km from Terran border

Site of the largest battle of the early war — Terran and Aetherian armies met and slaughtered each other for three days. The dead were never buried. They lie where they fell across kilometers of white against gray. At night, the bones glow phosphorescent — chemical reaction with the corruption, or souls still searching for peace. The Discarded say: good salvage, but leave before dark.

**Challenges:** Animated dead (corruption-driven, slow but relentless), competing scavengers, disorienting glow.
**Opportunities:** Weapons, armor, tools recoverable from the dead; orders, maps, diaries revealing forgotten history; bone samples valuable to scientists and alchemists.

---

### The Crater of Bones
**Location:** Central Wastes, ~75km from both borders

An Aetherian sky-city fell here in the early war — accident or act of war, never resolved. The crater is half a mile wide, a hundred feet deep, filled with the bones of tens of thousands and the wreckage of their city. The Aetherians wanted to recover them. The Terrans said no.

At night: manifestations of the dead appear. They don't attack, usually. They watch. They follow. They remember. Some visitors spend too long and never quite come back. Wreckage contains Aetherium crystals of extraordinary purity found nowhere else.

---

### The Sunken City of Aetherion
**Location:** Northern Wastes, buried under the dust. Multiple entrances, none safe.

Aetherion was the greatest city the world ever saw — the center of civilization, the heart of the Elder People, before the Cataclysm. It is preserved under the dust: buildings still standing, tables still set for meals, tools still laid out, offerings still on altars. And everywhere, the bones of those who stayed.

The deepest parts belong to the **Hollow**, who drift through the streets like memories. The crystals still hum and their song traps visitors in visions of the past. The corruption is strongest here and changes you slowly, without your noticing. Most people who enter do not come out. Some who come out are not the same.

**Opportunities:** Unmatched Elder technology; libraries and archives containing knowledge of who the Elders were and why they fell; answers to the question of what caused the Cataclysm.

---

### The Spire of Bones
**Location:** Eastern Wastes, center of the largest Abomination breeding ground

A tower of fused Abomination skeletons, built by no one, growing slowly over centuries. Rises hundreds of meters, visible for miles. Pulses green at night like a heartbeat. The Abominations that circle it are different — more organized, more intelligent, more aware. Something lives at the top. The Discarded have a name for it and do not speak it aloud.

---

### The Silent Market
**Location:** Constantly moving

The one place in the Gray Wastes where weapons are not allowed — where Terran, Aetherian, Discarded, and occasionally Deep Folk meet without killing each other. Moves by night, hides by day. Rules: no weapons, no fighting, no questions about origin. Trades everything: steel for crystal, information for coin, secrets for silence, Elder fragments, impossible maps. Run by the **Merchant Princes**, who know everything, have spies everywhere, and whose enforcement is absolute and unexplained.

---

## PART IV: OF THE DEEP PLACES

### The Deep Tunnels
Thousands of kilometers of passages beneath everything — some natural and improved by the Elder People, some perfectly artificial, some shaped by forces no one understands. Absolute darkness. The hum of deep crystals is constant. The Deep Iron use them to travel the entire world without seeing the sun, passing unseen beneath even the Gray Wastes.

**Dangers:** Darkness, labyrinthine navigation, Abominations, Hollow, collapse, flooding, gas pockets, total isolation.

---

### Stonehold (Deep Iron)
**Location:** Vast cavern system beneath the Ironspine Mountains. Pop: 60,000.

Built around an underground lake whose water is pure, filtered through miles of rock, untouched by the surface corruption. City carved into cavern walls — terraces, balconies, bridges lit by glow-moss and the lake's own faint luminescence. The Deep Iron are adapted: large eyes, pale skin, slow deliberate movement. The lake is sacred — it remembers everything that happened in the darkness. Those who drink too deeply never want to leave.

---

### The Forge of Stars
**Location:** Unknown. Somewhere in the deepest tunnels.

A legend — a workshop built by the Elder People in their greatest power, filled with tools that can shape matter itself, crystals holding starlight, machines transmuting one element to another. Lost in the Cataclysm. Every generation someone searches for it. Every generation they fail, or die, or return changed. The Deep Iron know it is real and do not look. They have seen what happens to those who seek the power of the Elders.

---

### The Deepest Places
Below even the Deep Tunnels, below anything with a name. Crystals grow thickest here, pulsing with light that is not light, humming with sound that is not sound. The Hollow gather and wait. The corruption is oldest and most aware here. No one who has gone to the deepest places has returned unchanged. Most have not returned.

---

## PART V: OTHER PLACES OF NOTE

### The Iron Monastery
**Location:** Hidden valley of the Ironspine Mountains. Accessible only by a single guarded pass.
**Population:** ~300 monks

The oldest dwelling-place of men — no one knows when it was built, not even the monks. The **Library** is the oldest and most complete knowledge repository in the world: records of the Elder People that the Elders themselves had lost, firsthand accounts of the Cataclysm, lists of names and places and things forgotten everywhere else. The monks watch, wait, and write. They share knowledge selectively. The cheese from their caves, aged for decades, is excellent and worth the trip on its own.

---

### The Floating Markets
Merit's greatest creation, still operating 500 years after his death. A caravan of tents, carts, and airships following seasons and wars and Abomination movements. Anything can be bought or sold here. Operated by the **Merchant Princes**, neutral and professional, whose security is absolute and whose methods are unknown.

---

### The Vault of Coins
**Location:** Unknown — supposedly the Gray Wastes

Merit's treasure, never found. Legends say it contains enough wealth to buy the world, enough secrets to destroy both sides. The monks suspect the treasure is smaller than legends claim and that Merit hid it not for its value but to leave behind a mystery — a reason for people to keep searching. They have been searching for five hundred years.

# ECHOES OF IRON AND AETHER — Game Design Reference
*A Tactical Turn-Based RPG | 330 Years of Endless War*

## 1. World Overview

For 330 years, the world has known no peace. What began as a dream of industrial progress has calcified into a nightmare of endless war. Two great powers stand locked in eternal struggle: **Great Terra** (the Iron Machine) and the **Aetherian Republic** (the Ascended). Between them lies the Gray Wastes — dead land of poisoned soil and toxic water, where the bones of millions lie beneath perpetual chemical fog. In the darkness, Abominations hunt by night, creatures born of war and mutation.

## 2. Factions

### Great Terra — The Iron Machine
**Motto:** "One World. One Work. One Purpose."

Totalitarian dictatorship founded in 1820 as a mining collective. The Iron Ministry rules from spires above the smog; workers labor in the Lower Rigs. Ideology: the natural world is flawed; humanity's destiny is fusion with industry. Goal: terraform the entire planet into a single efficient factory.

- **Military:** Iron Legions — heavily armored infantry, attrition tactics, chemical weapons
- **Technology:** Land-battleships, steam-powered walkers, industrial chemistry, Project Lazarus

### Aetherian Republic — The Ascended
**Motto:** "Ascension Through Purity."

Technocratic meritocracy founded in 1902. Originally a scientific colony, seceded from Terra and lifted their cities above the cloud layer. Ideology: order, genetics, intelligence. View Terrans as "polluted" sub-humans. Strive to ascend entirely to the skies.

- **Military:** High Guard — agile soldiers, lightweight alloys, electrical weaponry
- **Technology:** Airships, shock-batons, Arc Gauntlets, advanced radio, surgical treatments

### Abominations — Creatures of the Wastes
Creatures warped by mutation from the toxic Gray Wastes. First appeared 1985. Attack both Terra and Aetherian forces indiscriminately. Since 2005, an unofficial nightly ceasefire exists where soldiers from both sides fight together against them until dawn.

- **Known types:** Rock-Shell Crabs (massive, armored), Iron-Beak Vultures (aerial, metal-reinforced beaks)

### Project Lazarus
Great Terra's secret research program to animate the dead soldiers preserved in the Gray Wastes. If successful, Terra gains access to millions of undead soldiers requiring no food, sleep, or morale. Named for the biblical figure raised from death.

## 6. Combat Mechanics

**Turn Order:** Determined by Agility at start of each round. Higher Agility = earlier action. Random factor added. Dead characters skipped.

**Damage Formulas:**
- Physical: `Attack × Multiplier - Defense × 0.3–0.5`
- Tech: `Tech × Multiplier - Defense × 0.3–0.5`
- Critical: `Damage × 1.5` (chance based on Luck)
- Guard: `Damage × 0.5`

**Healing Formula:** `Base Amount + Tech × Multiplier`

**Hit Rate:** `Base Hit Rate - Target Evasion + Luck Factor` | Min: 5% | Max: 100% | Blinded: 0%

**Status Effect Mechanics:**
- **Stacking:** Corroded, Sickness, Lazarus Taint each increase effect per stack
- **On Expiry:** Adrenaline → Crash; Overcharged → System Shock
- **Permanent:** Toxin, Lazarus Taint, Death persist until cured by special means
- **Turn Processing:** DoT applies at turn start; duration decreases at turn end

# IRON & BLOOD — Game Concept Outline

## I. World Overview

Set in a **Piecraftian Dieselpunk** dystopia where human culture has ceased to evolve due to constant, widespread industrial warfare. Aesthetic defined by the "grease of fuel-powered machinery," noir ambience, and oppressive verticality of Art Deco architecture. The Machine Age pushed to its most pessimistic extreme — individual agency is often an illusion coerced by systemic corruption.

---

## II. 200-Year Surrounding History

A single point of divergence (POD) altered the trajectory of the modern era:

- **1825–1860 (The Discovery of Aetherium):** Miners in Wales discovered Aetherium, a glowing mineral with immense energy potential. Led to an accelerated "Second Industrial Revolution" — rapid mechanization of agriculture, rise of colossal refineries.

- **1860–1914 (The Rise of the Magnates):** Wealthy industrialists, inspired by Objectivist philosophy, built isolated utopian "Gilded Cities" to escape government control. Streamline Moderne design conveyed speed and technological prestige. Working class migrated to dense, disease-ridden urban centers to serve the "Heart Machines."

- **1914–1960 (The Perpetual Stalemate):** In 1914, a global conflict known as the **Trench Crusade** began, collapsing into permanent stalemate. For nearly 50 years the world has been locked in industrial warfare. Environment devastated by chemical and thermal hazards, creating vast "Toxic Jungles" of industrial waste.

- **Present Day (1960):** Society at a breaking point where Integrity (HP) is failing across all sectors. A noir landscape of self-destructive protagonists and systemic failure.

---

## III. Key Game Locations

### 1. Ironhaven Town *(The Starting Zone)*
A gritty, rain-slicked mining settlement inspired by Welsh industrial architecture. The town suffers from chronic mercury poisoning; the local population is exploited as cheap labor for the "Old Gearsmine." Players must fulfill Salvage Contracts to repair the town's failing Pressure Regulators.

### 2. The New Tower of Babel *(The Hub)*
A monolithic skyscraper serving as headquarters of the **Artificers' League**. Upper tiers are an Ottensian/Decopunk paradise of chrome finishes and geometric chevrons; the "catacombs" house the massive machines that power the city. Players visit the Workshop here to assemble components using a "Components First" philosophy.

### 3. No-Man's Trenches *(The Combat Zone)*
A desolate wasteland between the Gilded Cities — riveted armor, exposed cogs, endless trench warfare. A "Piecraftian" landscape where Combat Technicians use Percussive Maintenance to dismantle rogue automatons and heretic warbands.

### 4. The Rustwood Forest *(The Toxic Jungle)*
A wilderness created by ecocide during the **Seven Days of Fire** (an apocalyptic chemical event). Populated by mutant insects, protected by giant armored Ohm-like creatures. Field Medics are essential here, using Field Sutures and Serum (SRM) to counteract biological manipulation and toxicity.

---

## IV. RPG Mechanics & Progression

- **Task Resolution:** Fortune/Chance system (dice) for combat; Drama/Destiny system for narrative outcomes based on player choices.
- **Resource Management:** Players manage **Pressure (PRS)** for steam-powered skills and **Voltage** for high-output hardware.
- **Character Stance:** Players operate in Actor or Author stance, making moral choices around "harvesting" resources that lead to multiple game endings.
- **Progression:** Activity-based — skills like Precision and Resilience increase through exercised use rather than traditional leveling.

# IRON AND AETHER PART ZERO: THE FORGOTTEN AGES
*A Mythic History of the Elder Days*

---

## BOOK ONE: OF THE MAKING OF THE WORLD AND THE COMING OF THE DEEP CRYSTAL

### Chapter I: Of the Earth in Its Youth and the Star That Fell

Before the ages of men, a star fell from the firmament and smote the earth. Within that star dwelt metals forged in dying suns. Buried deep in the earth, cooling over long ages, its essences mingled with native rock and gave birth to **Aetherium** — the Deep Crystal, the Light of the Depths, the Stone That Remembers.

The crystal's atomic lattice arranged itself in a five-fold symmetry unknown to nature — a geometry existing only in the minds of mathematicians and the hearts of dying suns. Its properties:
- Could drink and store sunlight for centuries; a shard the size of a man's hand could hold the strength of a hundred men
- Released its stored energy in response to specific tones and harmonic vibrations
- When two crystals were hewn from the same mother-lode and attuned to each other, they remained entangled across any distance — energy and information passing between them through solid rock

---

### Chapter II: Of the First Men and Their Rising

After many ages there arose a people of greater wisdom than any before: the **Aethelings**, called in later days the Elder People or Precursors. Tall, fair, living five hundred years or more, death coming to them only "as a thief in the night, stealing upon them only when they had grown weary of the world."

They found the Aetherium and mastered it over patient generations. Their achievements:
- Built cities of crystal and light with towers piercing the clouds
- Learned to make Aetherium lessen the weight of objects, suspending platforms and dwellings in the air
- Built the **Grid of Resonance** — crystals buried at regular intervals across all lands, linked by entanglement, carrying power and knowledge like a network; one could speak across a thousand leagues as if standing side by side
- Learned to heal the body and lengthen life, reknitting bone and flesh in hours, restoring youth to the aged
- The most skilled transferred their minds into crystal entirely, becoming the **Crystalline Ones** (the Rememberers, the Wise) — living thoughts dwelling immortal in the heart of great crystals, advising the living with the wisdom of ages

---

### Chapter III: Of the Sundering of the Elder People

Division arose between two schools of thought regarding lesser men (the ancestors of all modern peoples, living then in caves with stone-tipped spears):

**The Elevationists (Grounders):** "Are they not our brothers? Let us teach them and lift them from their darkness."

**The Preservationists (Skyers):** "They are children in mind. If we give them the crystals they will make weapons of what should heal. Better to keep our knowledge and watch from afar."

The Skyers built floating cities and withdrew ever higher into clouds. The Grounders remained on earth, trading with and teaching the lesser men.

*This was the first sundering — echoing forward to the division of Terra and Aetheria.*

---

## BOOK TWO: OF THE CATACLYSM AND THE FALL OF THE ELDER PEOPLE

### Chapter I: Of the Great Undertaking and Its Failure

Among the Grounders arose a company of great daring and little wisdom. They planned to prove the Skyers wrong by focusing the energy of a thousand crystals on one point, creating an undeniable wonder. But they drew power from the Grid unevenly, creating stresses in the crystal lattice never intended.

One crystal somewhere in the deep places — perhaps flawed, perhaps weakened by age — shattered, releasing its stored energy as a burst of raw power. That burst, striking the entangled Grid, set off a chain reaction. Crystal after crystal resonated at unintended frequencies. The resonance grew, feeding on itself, spreading through the entanglement itself faster than sound.

**The Resonant Cascade:** Within hours, every Aetherium crystal on the continent released the stored energy of millennia simultaneously. Mountains leaped and fell. Crystal cities were thrown down. The sky burned. The great floating cities of the Skyers, deprived of lifting power, fell from the heavens like wounded birds.

---

### Chapter II: Of the Corruption of the Crystals

The Cascade did more than destroy. The perfect five-fold lattice was shattered in countless places — vacancies, dislocations, impurities. These defects reversed the crystals' properties:

- Where once they stored energy, now they **drained** it — drawing warmth, light, and life from all nearby
- Called **Void Crystals** or **Black Aetherium**: they glowed not with pure light but with a sickly darkness that hurt the eyes
- To touch one was to feel strength ebb; prolonged exposure caused sickness and death

Worse: the dark radiation they emitted fell upon living things and changed them.

---

### Chapter III: Of the Making of the Abominations

The dark radiation entered the cells of living creatures, disrupting their mitochondria — the engines of life — throwing them into confusion, causing them to burn too hot or too cold. The threads of heredity were broken in thousands of places. The body's repair systems, overwhelmed, began connecting broken threads incorrectly, creating patterns nature never intended. Master cells, awakened in confusion, built without plan — bone where flesh should be, flesh where bone should stand.

Thus the **Abominations** were made. Men became things that walked on twisted limbs. Beasts became worse than beasts, their minds broken into something that knew only pain and rage.

Most terrible: some creatures had fragments of corrupted crystal embedded in their living flesh. These fragments drained life from the host but also gave something back — a strange awareness, perception of energies beyond normal sight, connection to the dark network of corrupted crystals still pulsing deep in the earth.

These were the **Hollow**: not beasts (they have intelligence), not men (they have passed beyond humanity). Something new, born of the Cataclysm, waiting in deep places for purposes that living men cannot fathom.

---

### Chapter IV: Of the Long Winter and the Scattering of Peoples

After the Cataclysm came the Long Winter — generations without count. Dust from fallen cities blocked the sun. Snow fell in summer; ice crept down from mountains.

The surviving Aethelings scattered:
- **Deep into the earth** — tunnels and caverns where cold couldn't reach. Ancestors of the **Deep Iron Collective**.
- **To the highest peaks** — thin air but less corruption. Preserved crystal knowledge in fragments; ancestors of the **Aetherian Republic**.
- **Into the wasted lands** — learning to live among ruins, hunt Abominations, scavenge the dead. Ancestors of the Wanderers, Skyborn, Discarded, Merchant Princes.
- **To a single uncorrupted peak** — built a Monastery, meditated on the meaning of the Cataclysm, sought to understand the pride and folly that had brought their people low. These became the **Iron Monks**.

For forty thousand years the world lay in darkness. Memory of the Aethelings faded to legend, legend to myth, myth to forgotten things.

---

## BOOK THREE: OF THE SLOW AWAKENING AND THE COMING OF THE FOUNDERS

### Chapter I: Of the Long Climb Back

Slowly the world healed. The dust settled; ice retreated; corrupted crystals grew less potent as their stored energy bled into the earth. Men multiplied and spread. Tools of stone, then copper, then bronze, then iron. Villages, then towns, then cities.

In the mountains and deep places they began to find crystals again. Fear gave way to curiosity over generations. They learned: some crystals were safe, some were not. The pure crystals could be used for light, heat, labour. The corrupted must be avoided. Thus began the slow recovery of the old knowledge.

---

### Chapter II: Of Kaelen Ironhand, Founder of Terra

In the eighth century, in the western plains (rich soil, good iron mines), there arose a man of great strength and greater will: **Kaelen**, called Ironhand. The plains were divided among clans, each fighting neighbours over ore veins.

Kaelen said: *"Why do we die for a vein of ore when we might live for a kingdom of iron?"* He united the clans — some by words, some by marriage, some by defeat in battle. Then he built the **Great Foundry**: a city in itself, its smokestacks taller than any structure ever built, its forges hot enough to melt the hardest iron. Black smoke poured from it day and night — the beginning of the Haze.

Kaelen died in battle against a great host of Abominations. Buried beneath the Foundry. His successors carried on, expanding it over generations.

**Motto:** *"One World. One Work. One Purpose."*

---

### Chapter III: Of Seraphina, Who First Ascended

In the high eastern mountains dwelt descendants of those Skyers who had fled to the heights after the Cataclysm, still carrying fragments of the old crystal knowledge. In the ninth century, **Seraphina** was born among them.

She discovered what others had missed: properly tuned crystals, struck with precisely the right tones, would resonate in harmony, combining and amplifying their power. And she found that a fully energized tuned crystal could **lessen the weight of objects** bound to it — a hundred-pound stone could be lifted with the strength of ten; a platform could float upon the air.

She gathered a company of seekers, found a great crystal in a deep cave, and built a small platform bound to it. When they energized the crystal, it rose — lifting them above the mountains. Men below cried out in wonder. Seraphina called down: *"We seek only to rise above the earth's troubles, to find a place of peace where knowledge may be preserved."*

She wrote all she had learned in the **Codex of Ascension** and established a library. She died in a storm that struck her floating city, but her work endured. Her followers built more sky-havens, perfected crystal tuning, learned to draw power from the air, heal grievous wounds, and see at great distances through crystal lenses.

**Motto:** *"Ascension Through Purity."*

---

### Chapter IV: Of the Iron Monk and the Mountain Temple

Dwelling apart from nations, upon a lonely peak, a company descended from those Aethelings who had retreated to meditate on the Cataclysm. Their founder — true name unrecorded, called simply the **Iron Monk** — had seen in visions the pride and folly that had brought down the Elder People: their dependence on crystals, their belief in their own wisdom.

He taught a different way: *"The mountain does not move for the wind. The stone does not break for the hammer. Let us become as the mountain and the stone, drawing strength not from without but from within."*

His followers developed arts of body and mind that seemed miraculous — striking with force that bent iron, moving with speed that arrows missed, enduring cold and heat and hunger that would kill ordinary men.

*"The crystals failed our ancestors. We will not repeat their mistake."*
*"Cities can be destroyed. We will build only ourselves."*

Thus was the **Iron Monastery** founded.

---

### Chapter V: Of Merit, Who First Traded

In the lands between mountains and plains, a woman named **Merit** was born to a wandering tribe of traders. She saw that petty trade could become something greater: *"Let us establish regular routes, make agreements with leaders, become the people to whom all others come when they need what they cannot make."*

She travelled farther than any of her people — to the Foundry itself, to the sky-havens. She learned many languages and customs, made agreements, established the first trade routes (marked by cairns and waystations), set fair prices, and created a system of credit. Her descendants built a network of trade linking all peoples. They grew rich, and powerful — for information travels with goods, and those who control trade often know more than kings.

Thus were the **Merchant Princes** founded.
**Motto:** *"Every problem has a price."*

---

### Chapter VI: Of Cobble, Who United the Deep Folk

Deep beneath the mountains, descendants of those who had fled underground after the Cataclysm had adapted to their dark world — large eyes, sharp hearing, knowing every tunnel for leagues, farming fungi, herding blind fish, preserving Elder relics. But divided into many small tribes, fighting each other in the darkness.

In the thirteenth century, a leader called **Cobble** (solid and reliable as a foundation stone) went from tribe to tribe: *"The darkness is wide enough for all. The tunnels are long enough for all. Why do we fight when we might work together?"* He persuaded them to send representatives to a great council in a cavern large enough to hold them all. They agreed to share tunnels, pool knowledge, and defend each other against Abominations.

Thus was the **Deep Iron Collective** founded.

---

## BOOK FOUR: OF THE SEEDS OF WAR

### Chapter I: Of the Rediscovery of the Great Crystals

As centuries passed, Terra expanded its foundries and mines. Aetheria built more sky-havens and perfected its crystal arts. And in the mountains, miners from both sides began to find great deposits of **pure Aetherium**, untouched by the Cataclysm's corruption, glowing with the old light.

Terra wanted them for power. Aetheria wanted them for knowledge. In 1855, they signed the **Treaty of Supply**: Terra provides food and coal to mountain settlements; Aetheria mines and refines Aetherium and sends it down to the plains.

---

### Chapter II: Of the Straining of the Bond

For decades the arrangement held. Terra grew fat on Aetherium power; the Republic grew wise on Terra's food. But Terra's hunger grew, and its demands with it — triple the output, strip the mountains of every crystal they contained.

Aetheria refused: *"The crystals are a trust, a legacy of the Elder People. If we take too much too fast, we may trigger another Cataclysm."*

Terra scoffed: *"Old wives' tales. Superstition. They are resources, nothing more."*

Other grievances accumulated: Terra's smoke drifted east, darkening mountain skies. Factory waste poisoned rivers flowing toward the mountains, killing fish, sickening children. The Republic protested. Terra ignored them.

---

### Chapter III: Of the Breaking

In 1900, Terra issued the **Unification Ultimatum**: *"Surrender control of the mines, or be integrated into the Foundry."* Not an offer — a threat.

The Republic refused: *"We are a free people, and we will not yield."*

Terra prepared for war: land-battleships, steam-powered walkers, chemical weapons capable of choking whole cities. Aetheria sealed the passes with automated crystal turrets, trained the High Guard in crystal weapons, sent word to the Merchant Princes for supplies.

In 1902, the first shots were fired. The **Skyward Schism** had begun.

*"And thus the world was divided, as it had been divided in the elder days, as it would be divided in all the days to come — until perhaps, in some future age, a new founder might arise to heal the ancient wound."*

## GAZETTEER SUPPLEMENT

### Great Terra

**The Hive (The Super-Foundry)**
Capital of Great Terra. From a distance appears as a low mountain range of iron and soot, its peaks crowned by the Ministry spires. The Haze — a perpetual smoke canopy — blocks the sun in the lower districts; only furnace-glow and gas lamps provide light.

Three layers:
- *The Spires:* Polished steel and black iron; Iron Ministry, military command, elite residences. Windows that actually open.
- *The Mid-Levels:* Workshops, barracks, commercial districts; skilled workers; air thick but breathable.
- *The Lower Rigs:* Nearest the original Foundry. Heat oppressive, air toxic, noise deafening. Workers rarely see the sky; lifespans measured in decades. A labyrinth of rusting catwalks, leaking pipes, overworked machinery.

Notable features: Kaelen's Tomb (location a closely guarded Ministry secret; Ironheart said to await Terra's greatest need); The Ministry Archive (schematics for every machine ever built); The Recruitment Pits (where children as young as eight are taken for Iron Legion training).

**The Under-District**
Network of tunnels, chambers, and laboratories carved into the bedrock beneath the Hive, accessible only through hidden entrances guarded by the Ministry's most loyal troops.

Notable features: The Lazarus Chamber (central laboratory; rumoured to contain vats of preserved bodies awaiting animation); The Black Vault (corrupted Aetherium crystals recovered from the Gray Wastes, under study); The Silence Cells (where failed experiments are kept — or disposed of).

### Aetherian Republic

**SkyHaven Harbor**
Capital of the Aetherian Republic. Platform of crystalline steel and aether-glass suspended beneath a great cluster of Aetherium crystals at ~3,000m. Multiple levels connected by spiral ramps and crystal lifts. Upper levels (closest to the lifting crystals) reserved for the High Council, top scholars, finest artisans — airy, open, with gardens, recycling fountains, and panoramic cloud views.

Notable features: The Seraphina Spire (tallest structure; houses the High Council; uppermost chamber holds the original Codex of Ascension in Seraphina's own hand); The Library of Whispers; The Resonance Chamber (spherical room lined with tuned Aetherium for resonance experiments); The Sky-Docks (where airships are built, maintained, and launched).

**The Library of Whispers**
Built into a peak so remote and high that even hardy Abominations rarely approached it. Now the Republic's archive, memory, and conscience. Tunnels and chambers carved into living rock, shelves holding not only books but crystal data-slabs, preserved Precursor artifacts, and personal journals of generations of scholars.

Notable features: The Precursor Vault (most complete collection of Precursor texts in existence; highest-ranking scholars only); The Hall of Founders (statues and memorials from Seraphina to present); The Echo Chamber (tuned crystals that replay recorded sounds — including, it is said, the voices of the founders themselves).

---

### The Gray Wastes

**Overview**
What was once a fertile valley is now a dead zone: black lifeless soil, chemical sludge water, yellowish-gray fog smelling of decay and burnt metal. Nothing natural lives here — nothing born before the poisoning. A landscape of ruins, bones, abandoned village skeletons, trenches, craters, and destroyed vehicle hulks. Beneath the surface, preserved by the toxic environment: the bodies of millions.

By day, relatively traversable (patrols, Discarded scavengers, Abominations mostly sleeping). By night, belongs to the Abominations — hence the Armistice of Silence.

Notable features: The Crater of Bones (site of a fallen Aetherian sky-city; inhabitants fused with crystal and steel; said to be haunted); The Sunken City (Aetherion, beneath the Wastes); The Abomination Breeding Grounds (areas of high crystal corruption where Abominations are spontaneously generated from corrupted soil + chemicals + remains of the dead); The Silent Market (neutral night market, weapons prohibited; Abominations, for unknown reasons, do not approach).

**The Sunken City (Aetherion)**
Capital of the Elder People, buried beneath millennia of sediment and Cataclysm debris. Parts accessible through tunnels; explorers report wonders and terrors beyond imagining.

Notable features: The Crystal Spire (tallest surviving structure; lights reported in upper chambers with no natural source); The Resonance Core (where the great Grid crystal once stood; now a twisted mass of corrupted crystal; those who approach too closely experience visions of the Cataclysm); The Hall of Rememberers (where the Crystalline Ones once dwelt; crystal matrices still line the walls, dark and silent — some say only sleeping); Deep Tunnels connecting to the Deep Iron Collective's realm.

---

### The Mountains

**The Ironspine Mountains**
Ancient range, older than the Cataclysm, older than the Aethelings. Iron-rich dark rock giving a forbidding, metallic appearance. Steep, treacherous, passes impassable much of the year. Also rich in Aetherium — the geological forces that thrust up the mountains exposed deep crystal veins. Dotted with ruins of ancient mines (both Precursor and modern) and hidden Deep Iron refuges.

**Windward Pass**
Strategic chokepoint. Sealed by Aetheria in 1895; Terra's 1915 invasion crushed here. Now a fortified zone — front line shifting back and forth by mere meters across decades.

Notable features: The Iron Gate (massive Terran bunker at western entrance; artillery, barracks, field hospital); The Crystal Wall (Aetherian defensive line at eastern entrance; crystal-tipped stakes and automated turrets); No-Man's-Land (central section; so many shifts of the front line that soil is a jumble of trenches, craters, debris — nowhere in the world richer in blood); The Armistice Cave (natural cavern midway through, used by soldiers from both sides during the night ceasefire).

**The Crystal Labyrinth**
Natural formation deep within the Ironspines, accessible only through hidden entrances. Caverns lined with Aetherium crystals grown over millions of years — columns, arches, delicate filigrees catching light and scattering it in a thousand colors. Beautiful. Deadly. Crystals unstable; a wrong step triggers cascades of falling shards. Deeper passages corrupted; Abominations adapted to the crystal environment. A true maze — many who enter never find their way out.

**The Iron Monastery**
Not a single building but a complex clinging to a peak that rises above the clouds. Built of the mountain's own stone, almost invisible from a distance. Only the faint glow of the uncorrupted crystal in its central courtyard betrays its presence.

---

### Underground

**The Deep Tunnels**
An entire underground world stretching hundreds of miles. Natural caverns, Precursor excavations, and tunnels carved by the Deep Iron over millennia.

Upper levels (relatively safe): Deep Iron settlements — towns around underground lakes, fungal farms, forges heated by volcanic vents. Air warm and damp, lit by bioluminescent fungi and occasional Aetherium crystals.

Notable features: The Great Cavern (largest known cavern; the Deep Iron capital Stonehold is built here); The Fungal Forests (vast groves of giant fungi, some bioluminescent); The Volcanic Forges (exceptional-quality metal, prized by surface traders); The Silent Passages (said to be haunted by ghosts of the Elder People; the Deep Iron warn everyone away).

**The Forge of Stars**
Greatest of the Precursor legends. A complex of chambers built around a volcanic vent where the heat of the earth's core could be harnessed for the most demanding work. Here (it is said) the first Aetherium crystals were shaped, the first sky-ships built, the Crystalline Ones created. Lost in the Cataclysm. No one has found it. No one who truly knows what it is would want to — the Deep Iron have seen what happens to those who seek the power of the Elders.

# ON THE MATTER OF THE WORLD
*Being a Complete Geographical Account of the Known World, as Compiled by the Cartographers of the Iron Monastery*

---

## PART I: OF THE WORLD IN GENERAL

The world as known in 2152 is a world of scars. The Elder People shaped it to their liking, then nearly destroyed it. The Cataclysm poisoned vast stretches of land, creating the Gray Wastes. The war carved new wounds — trenches, craters, the remains of cities that once floated in the sky.

Three great mountain ranges define the known world: the **Ironspine Mountains** in the east, the **Jagged Hills** in the west, and the **Serrated Peaks** in the far north. Between them lie the valleys and plains that have been fought over for two hundred and fifty years.

Two great factions dominate: Great Terra in the west, the Aetherian Republic in the east. Between them, the Gray Wastes — a poisoned valley that was once the heart of civilization and is now its grave.

And beneath it all, another world entirely: the Deep Tunnels, where the Deep Iron dwell and older things stir in the darkness.

*Footnote from the Cartographers: The monks have attempted to calculate the exact size of the known world. The world keeps changing — mountains crumble, wastes expand, new tunnels open. The only constant is that everywhere is approximately three days' travel from somewhere you'd rather not be.*

---

## PART II: OF GREAT TERRA

Great Terra occupies the western portion of the known world, stretching from the Western Sea to the edge of the Gray Wastes. The sky is never truly clear. Smoke from countless factories creates a permanent haze, filtering sunlight to a sickly orange. At night, the glow of forges lights the clouds from below. The land is exhausted — centuries of mining have stripped the richest ores, rivers run thick with industrial waste, forests were cut for fuel and never replanted. Terra survives not on its own resources but on what it can pull from the earth before the earth gives out.

### Chapter I: The Hive

*"The Hive is not a city. It is an argument, made manifest in iron and stone and the bones of generations. Terra's answer to every question the world has ever asked, and the answer is always: more."*

**Location:** At the confluence of three rivers in western Terra, on ground tested and confirmed free of corruption before the first stone was laid.
**Population:** ~2.5 million (no accurate count in decades; the Lower Rigs alone could hold twice that, packed as they are).

**Three Layers:**
- *The Spires:* Above the smoke, almost breathable air, almost natural light. Masters, engineers, administrators, Iron Ministry. Buildings of iron filigree reaching toward a sky they can barely see. From their windows, a machine functioning correctly — they do not look too closely at the parts.
- *The Mid-Levels:* Layer upon layer of factories, forges, assembly lines. Craftsmen, technicians, skilled laborers — work here, live here, die here, replaced by their children. The hammering, hissing, grinding becomes the only silence you can bear.
- *The Lower Rigs:* Endless cramped corridors, flickering lights, steam pipes that burst as often as they work. Recycled air heavy with unwashed bodies and industrial lubricant. Minimum-nutrient food. The people here do not look up. There is nothing to see.

**Notable Locations:**
- *The Great Foundry:* Heart of the Hive, first structure built when the clans united. Never stopped working. A thousand years of continuous metal flow — weapons, armor, tools, the city's own bones. Chimneys rise higher than the Spires, turning the sky orange for miles.
- *The Iron Ministry Complex:* Fortress within a fortress. Walls within walls, checkpoints within checkpoints. No one knows what happens in the deepest chambers. No one asks.
- *Kaelen's Tomb:* Beneath the Great Foundry, sealed a thousand years. The Founder, hammer Ironheart on his chest, still warm, still waiting. The Ministry has considered opening it, then remembered: waking a man like Kaelen from a thousand-year nap is a very good way to get hit with a hammer.
- *The Under-District:* Beneath even the Lower Rigs, beneath the city's foundations, a place that does not officially exist. Project Lazarus, and other works. Workers here do not speak of what they do. The walls speak for them, in the language of screams that never quite fade.

**Defenses:** Black iron walls, twenty meters high, ten thick. Artillery on every level. Automated turrets tracking anything that moves. Fifty thousand standing garrison, hundred thousand reservists deployable within hours.

**Exports:** Heavy weapons, industrial machinery, chemical compounds, soldiers.
**Imports:** Aetherium crystals, food (the Hive cannot feed itself), salvage from the Gray Wastes.

*Cheese: Adequate but uninspired. Hard, yellow, nutritious, exactly as exciting as that description suggests.*

*Footnote: The Hive has a crime rate of approximately zero percent. Not because there is no crime, but because the definition of crime has been carefully adjusted. Stealing from the Ministry is crime. The Ministry taking everything you have is taxation. The distinction, the Ministry explains, is obvious to anyone with a proper understanding of civic responsibility.*

### Chapter II: Outlying Terran Settlements

**Ironhaven** (Pop: 120,000)
Mining city built into the Jagged Hills. Produces most of the raw ore feeding the Hive's furnaces. Terraces cut into the mountain, connected by funicular railways. Mines extend miles into the earth — iron, coal, and occasionally older things. People are hard, proud, fiercely protective of their contribution to the Great Work. The Ministry encourages this pride. It makes them easier to manage.
*Cheese: Smoked for preservation through long winters. Locals consider the flavor perfect. Visitors find it overwhelming.*

**Foundry** (Pop: 80,000)
Factory city: weapons only. No mines, no farms, no reason to exist except rifles, cannon, and ammunition. Workers are specialists watched constantly, because a skilled weapons maker who defects would be a disaster. Well-paid, well-fed, well-housed by Terran standards.
*Cheese: None. Ministry rations only — nutritious and flavorless.*

**Greasewater** (Pop: 45,000)
Terra's only significant port on the Western Sea. Ironclads, gunboats, experimental vessels. Rough, even by Terran standards. Sailors from dozens of factions pass through with foreign customs, foreign diseases, foreign money. The one place in Terra where you can find non-Terran goods, and where you can leave by ship if you must.
*Cheese: Salty from sea air. Sailors love it.*

---

### Chapter III: Terran Military Installations

**Fort Iron** (Garrison: 15,000)
Primary Terran fortress at the edge of the Gray Wastes, guarding the western approach to Windward Pass. Attacked a hundred times, besieged a dozen, never taken. Built into a hill with layered defensive walls, year's worth of supplies underground, artillery covering every approach. From the highest walls on a rare clear day: the wastes, the mountains, and things moving in the wastes. The soldiers have learned not to look closely.

**The Trenches of Windward Pass**
Not one location but a network of fortifications along the mountain pass. Hell — mud, blood, rats, constant death. Thirty-day rotations; few survive three.

*Night in the Trenches:* When the sun goes down, Abominations rise. Terran and Aetherian soldiers — enemies by day — become allies by night. They fight together against things that care nothing for flags or factions. They share rations, fire, names. When the sun rises, they go back to trying to kill each other. Neither side speaks of this. Their leaders do not know, or pretend not to know.

## PART III: OF THE AETHERIAN REPUBLIC

The Aetherian Republic occupies the eastern heights of the Ironspine Mountains. The sky over Aetheria is clear — cleaner than anywhere else in the world. Mountains rise above Terra's smoke, above the haze of the Gray Wastes, into air so pure it almost hurts. At night, stars so bright you can read by them. The land is harsh — steep, narrow-valleyed, thin-soiled. The Aetherians survived not by conquering their environment but by learning to work with it.

### Chapter I: The Sky-Cities

**SkyHaven Harbor** (Pop: 180,000)
First sky-city, still the greatest. Hangs 3,000m above Mount Aether on threads of crystal that look too thin to hold anything but somehow hold everything. Central platform of crystal and steel, one kilometer across, with radiating spokes: Scholars' Quarter (libraries, academies), Artisans' Spoke (open workshops), Garden Ring (hanging gardens cascading green against blue sky). The harbor on the eastern edge: airships dock, merchants gather, the world passes through. From any point: mountains below, sky above, other sky-cities around like ships on an ocean of air.
*Cheese: Soft, fresh, delivered daily from mountain dairies. Light, creamy, utterly delicious — the sort that makes you understand why people fight wars over things that are not cheese.*

**Seraphina Spire** (Pop: 45,000)
Tallest structure in the world — a needle of crystal and steel piercing the sky from its own platform, twelve kilometers east of SkyHaven. Research facility, monument, pilgrimage site. At its heart: the **Codex of Ascension** in crystal and silence — changing subtly over time, passages appearing that weren't there before, questions answered somehow from somewhere. Observation deck at the peak, a thousand meters above the platform: the curve of the world, the darkness of space, stars close enough to touch. Some visitors never want to come down.
*Cheese: None produced on site. Visitors bring their own.*

**Other Sky-Cities:**
- *Crystal-Haven:* Dedicated to crystal mining; hovers above richest veins; workers lowered on cables
- *Star-View:* Astronomy and atmospheric research; platforms covered with telescopes and instruments
- *Garden-Ring:* Hydroponic agriculture feeding the entire Republic; hanging gardens famous throughout the world
- *Forge-Spire:* Precision manufacturing; forges burn clean on crystal power; no smoke in the clear air

---

### Chapter II: Mountain Cities

**Stonehold (Aetherian)** (Pop: 90,000)
Built into the eastern face of Mount Stone on cliff terraces connected by stairways and funiculars climbing a vertical kilometer. People are miners and masons, engineers and architects — the city is their masterpiece, every building fitted to its site. Beneath it: the richest crystal mines in the Republic, dangerous but essential. Without Stonehold's crystals, the sky-cities would fall.
*Cheese: Aged in natural caves. Sharp, complex, highly prized throughout the Republic.*

**High Peak** (Pop: 25,000)
Highest permanently inhabited settlement in the world at 6,000m. Inhabitants are born adapted — larger lungs, thicker blood. Research station and monitoring post watching the northern wastes. From here on clear days: Serrated Peaks to the north, Gray Wastes to the west, the entire Republic spread below like a map. At night: aurora filling half the sky with shifting light.
*Cheese: None. Altitude makes cheesemaking impossible. Everything imported at great expense.*

---

### Chapter III: Aetherian Military Installations

**Fort Sky-Watch** (Garrison: 8,000)
Primary Aetherian fortress on the edge of the Gray Wastes, guarding the eastern approach to Windward Pass. Never fallen. Walls of stone and crystal gleaming in the sun; batteries covering the pass below. From the highest tower: across the Gray Wastes to the distant smudge of Fort Iron, the glint of Terran artillery on clear days. The soldiers have learned not to look too long.

**The Watcher Stations**
Small outposts (teams of a few observers each) scattered along the Republic's borders, monitoring Terran movements and Abomination activity. Six-month rotations in isolation. Some emerge unchanged. Most emerge changed. A few do not emerge at all.

## PART IV: OF THE GRAY WASTES

What remains of the valley that was once the heart of the Elder People's civilization. The most fertile, most populous, most advanced region in the world — before the Cataclysm poisoned everything. Rivers ran thick with corruption, then stopped. Forests went gray, then black, then crumbled to dust. Cities emptied, collapsed, sank into the earth as if the ground was trying to swallow them.

The people died in waves — first the old, then the young, then everyone else. In the streets, in their homes, in tunnels they dug trying to escape. Their bones are still there. Millions of them, buried under gray dust, preserved by the very poisons that killed them.

**Size:** ~300km north-south, ~150km east-west (shifting as corruption expands/contracts)
**Climate:** Hot by day, freezing by night; dust storms without warning; thin toxic air (hours without protection = permanent damage)
**Terrain:** Flat, mostly; remnants of ancient cities rising like broken teeth; craters from fallen sky-cities; cracked ground hiding tunnels and chambers that were once streets
**Color:** Everything gray — dust, sky, ruins, bones. Only color: Abominations (sickly greens and blues) and corrupted crystals pulsing deep underground
**Sky:** Always overcast; dust perpetually suspended; dim twilight even at noon

---

### Chapter II: Regions of the Wastes

**The Bone Fields (Southern Wastes)**
Site of the largest battle of the early war — three days of open combat, slaughter from both sides, dead never buried (too many; ground already poisoned). Bones stretching for kilometers, white against gray. Soldiers' bones, civilians' bones, horses and machines and things that were neither. Wind whistling through them.

*Dangers:* Some dead rise, animated by corruption — slow and mindless but relentless. Scavengers compete for the remains.
*Resources:* Weapons, armor, tools, supplies carried by the dead — much still functional.

**The Crater of Bones (Central Wastes)**
A sky-city fell here in the early war — accident or act of war, never settled. Half a mile wide, a hundred feet deep, filled with tens of thousands of bones mixed with city wreckage. The Aetherians wanted to bury them; the Terrans controlled the valley and said no. That was a hundred years ago.

*Dangers:* Haunted — literally. Manifestations appear at night, usually. They do not attack, usually. They simply watch.
*Resources:* Aetherium crystals of extraordinary purity, advanced technology, personal effects of the dead.

**The Sunken City of Aetherion (Northern Wastes)**
Beneath the Wastes, buried under centuries of dust and poison and bones, lies the capital of the Elder People. Still there. The corruption preserved it as surely as it killed it. Buildings still standing. Tables still set. Tools still laid out. Offerings still on altars. And everywhere, the bones of those who stayed.

Multiple entrances — tunnels from the surface, collapsed sections, passages connecting to the Deep Tunnels. None are safe. All are watched.

*The Hollow* drift through the deepest streets like memories, like things that have forgotten what it meant to be alive but remember what it meant to be human. They do not bother visitors, usually. The city itself is dangerous enough without them.

*Dangers:* The Hollow; the crystals (still singing, can trap the unwary in visions); collapsed structures; residual corruption that seeps into skin and lungs before you notice; the crushing feeling that you are trespassing on sacred ground and the dead are watching.
*Resources:* Everything — the Elder People's technology, purer crystals, stronger metals, more advanced devices. A single successful expedition can make a scavenger rich for life. Most expeditions are not successful.

**The Spire of Bones (Eastern Wastes)**
A tower of fused skeletons, built by no one, growing slowly over centuries. The heart of Abomination territory. Hundreds, thousands of Abominations drawn from across the wastes — circling it, crawling over it, adding their bones when they die. The Spire's influence suppresses their aggression toward each other and focuses it outward. No one goes near it. Whatever lives at the top watches.

**The Silent Market (Location Varies)**
The one place in the Gray Wastes where weapons are not allowed. Where Terrans and Aetherians and Discarded and even, occasionally, the Deep Folk can meet without killing each other. Moves constantly — a caravan of tents and carts and makeshift stalls, traveling by night and hiding by day.

*Rules:* No weapons. No fighting. No questions about where things come from. Trade what you have for what you need. Leave. Do not look back.
*What's traded:* Everything — Terran steel for Aetherian crystal, information for coin, secrets for silence, Elder technology fragments, maps of places that should not exist, crystals that hum at a frequency that makes your teeth ache.
*The Merchant Princes* run it — neutral, professional, utterly without mercy for those who break the rules. Security absolute. No one knows how they enforce it.

## PART V: OF THE DEEP PLACES

### Chapter I: The Deep Tunnels

Thousands of kilometers, connecting locations across the known world. Varieties: natural caves widened by the Elder People; perfectly smooth artificial passages; places shaped by forces that are not mining or construction or anything that makes sense. Absolutely dark. The darkness presses in, heavy as water, hungry as a beast.

Not silent: water drips, rocks shift, things move. And always in the background — the hum. The crystal hum. The sound of the earth itself, the music of deep places.

---

### Chapter II: The Deep Iron Collective

Descendants of everyone who ever fled into the tunnels — miners escaping corruption, refugees fleeing wars, exiles fleeing their own people. A thousand years of evolving in isolation.

*Appearance:* Pale skin, large eyes, thin bodies — plus harder, tougher, more resilient than surface-dwellers. The dark makes you strong, or it kills you.

*Culture:* No single government. Collectives bound by mutual agreement and shared purpose. Decisions by consensus (which takes years). Rushing leads to mistakes; mistakes in the deep are fatal.

**Stonehold (Deep Iron)** (Pop: 60,000)
Largest Deep Iron city, built around an underground lake in a vast cavern beneath the Ironspine Mountains. The lake's water is pure — filtered through miles of rock, protected from surface corruption. City carved into cavern walls: terraces, balconies, bridges, lit by glow-moss and the lake's faint luminescence. The Deep Iron believe the water remembers everything that has ever happened in the darkness, and that when you drink it, you become part of that memory.

Only one entrance — easily defended. The Deep Iron have no enemies who can reach them here.
*Cheese: Aged in absolute darkness for a hundred years. The Deep Iron claim it is exquisite. Surface-dwellers find it challenging.*

**The Forge of Stars** (Legendary; Location Unknown)
Somewhere in the deepest tunnels, buried under miles of rock, guarded by traps and puzzles and things that should not exist: the Elder People's greatest workshop. Tools that can shape matter itself. Crystals that hold the light of stars. Machines that transmute one element to another. Lost in the Cataclysm. Stories never died. Every generation someone searches; every generation someone fails or dies in the attempt. The Deep Iron do not look. They know better.

---

### Chapter III: The Deepest Places

Below even the Deep Tunnels, below anything with a name. Crystals grow thickest here — pulsing with light that is not light, humming with sound that is not sound. The Hollow gather and wait. The corruption is oldest and most aware here. No one who has gone to the deepest places has returned unchanged. Most have not returned.

*The monks do not speak of these places. They simply note, in their records, that some doors should remain forever closed.*

## PART VI: OTHER PLACES OF NOTE

**The Iron Monastery**
The oldest dwelling-place of men. No one knows when it was built — not even the monks. Hidden in a remote Ironspine valley, accessible only by a single guarded pass. Built into the mountain, carved from living rock, designed to last forever. It has lasted this long. The Library: oldest and most complete knowledge collection in the world — records of the Elder People that the Elders themselves had lost, Cataclysm accounts written by survivors, lists of things forgotten everywhere else.

*Cheese: Excellent. Made in caves behind the monastery. Traded for stories, news, anything that adds to the monks' understanding of the world. The monks are hungry for information. They never seem to get enough.*

**The Floating Markets**
Merit's greatest creation, still operating five hundred years after her death. A caravan of tents, carts, and airships traveling the world without ever staying in one place, following seasons and wars and Abomination movements. Anything can be bought or sold here. Run by the Merchant Princes — neutral, professional, utterly without mercy for those who break the rules.

**The Vault of Coins** (Location Unknown; supposedly the Gray Wastes)
Merit's treasure, never found. Rumored to contain enough wealth to buy the world, enough secrets to destroy both sides. The monks believe the treasure is smaller than legends suggest and that Merit hid it not for its value but to leave behind a mystery — a reason for people to keep searching. Five hundred years of searching. Five hundred more to come.

## BOOK ONE: OF THE ELDER DAYS

### The Making of the World
The earth was young, and for millions of years nothing much happened. Continents drifted. Mountains rose and eroded. Then a star fell — not a large star, merely a fragment of something greater that had reached the end of its journey. It buried itself in the bones of the world and waited. The place where it struck would become, in later ages, the Gray Wastes. At the time, it was merely a smoking crater and a great deal of dead wildlife.

### The Coming of Men and the Finding of Crystal
Men spread across the earth in waves: some went west (becoming Great Terra), some east into the mountains (becoming the Aetherian Republic), some down into the dark places (becoming something else entirely). And some, digging in the mountains for metals, found something they had no name for.

The crystals were beautiful. They caught the light and threw it back in colors that had no business existing. They hummed — a sound not quite a sound, a vibration from somewhere behind your teeth.

The men who found them did what men always do with something beautiful and inexplicable: they fought over them, worshipped them, and tried to figure out what they were for. It took a very long time to understand that the question was not what the crystals were *for* but what they *were*. By the time they figured it out, it was far too late.

*Footnote: The Elder People eventually named these crystals "Aetherium" — roughly "light-stuff" or "that which shines." It did not mean "that which will eventually turn the valley into a poisoned hellscape and give birth to creatures that should not exist." This is considered, by modern scholars, to have been an oversight.*

### The Sundering of the Elder People
The one pattern from which humanity has never successfully deviated: gather enough people in one place, give them enough to eat, let them sit still for a generation or two, and the first thing they will do is find reasons to hate each other.

**Western Elders** built downward, into the earth — great mines, vast foundries, crystals shaped into tools of industry. They looked at a mountain and saw raw material.

**Eastern Elders** built upward, toward the sky — cities carved into peaks, stars studied, crystals made to sing. They looked at a mountain and saw a temple.

For a long time this was merely a difference of opinion. They traded iron for crystal and everyone agreed it was an excellent arrangement that would surely last forever. The crystals, deep in the earth, hummed quietly and waited.

### The Great Undertaking and the Cataclysm
The Western Elders' mines were running dry. The solution: dig deeper. The Eastern Elders said perhaps one should not dig too deeply into places where crystals had grown since before men walked the earth. The Western Elders said this was the sort of thinking that kept people living in caves.

So they dug deeper. And deeper. And deeper.

*Footnote: There is a saying among the monks: "The earth does not give up its secrets willingly. It must be asked properly, with respect, and even then it may lie." The Western Elders did not ask. They took. The earth kept receipts.*

### The Corruption of the Crystals
The crystals at the deepest levels were different — the color of old blood, humming at a frequency that made teeth ache and thoughts turn in unintended directions. The miners brought them up anyway. That was their job.

The corruption spread slowly, invisibly. A smelter here, an engineer there — they grew quieter, stopped laughing, stopped complaining about working conditions. When they started eating the crystal dust, it was already too late.

### The Making of the Abominations
The first Abominations were men. The things that haunt the Gray Wastes were not *created* by the Cataclysm — they *were* the Cataclysm, wearing the faces of the people who caused it.

Crystal dust worked into blood, bones, brains. It replaced things. It added things. It changed the rules.

The first sign was always the eyes: deep dark, the dark of places where light has never reached. Then the skin hardened, taking on a crystalline sheen. Then the hunger started — for crystal, for the life-force crystal could drain from living things, for the warmth they could no longer feel themselves.

When the first city fell — a mining outpost called Deepreach, three miles underground — the survivors who made it to the surface brought the corruption with them. They didn't mean to. But the crystal was in them now, and the crystal was patient, and the crystal had plans.

*Footnote: The Hollow, who dwell in the deepest ruins to this day, are what happens when the process is allowed to complete itself. They are not quite alive and not quite dead, and they remember everything — including what it felt like to be human. This is, the monks say, their punishment.*

### The Long Winter and the Scattering
The Cataclysm was not quick. It was a slow unraveling across generations. The crystals, once awakened, did not stop. They spread through the earth like poison through blood. Crops failed. Animals died or fled or changed.

Survivors scattered to their refuges:
- **West**, to lands where iron in the soil resisted the crystal's spread → became Great Terra
- **East**, to highest mountains where thin cold air slowed corruption's progress → became the Aetherian Republic
- **Down**, into deepest tunnels the corruption had not yet reached → became the Deep Iron
- **Some stayed** — could not run, or would not, or had already changed too much to want to → became the Discarded, the Hollow, the things that wait in ruins and remember

### The Slow Awakening
The world heals slowly. The monks are certain of this because they have watched it happen.

A thousand years for the Gray Wastes to stop spreading. Five hundred more for the first plants to grow back — tough, bitter plants that fight for every inch. Three hundred more for animals to return, changed and harder and meaner. Then survivors emerged and found the crystals had stopped growing. Reached some limit. Pushed back by the earth, somehow.

Or so it seemed. The crystals, deep in the earth, hummed quietly and waited. They had learned patience from the stars, and the stars had taught them well.

### The Founders

**Kaelen Ironhand** — Not the sort of person you'd expect to unite the western clans. Difficult. Opinionated. Expressed himself, loudly and at length, usually while hitting things with a hammer. But he understood two things no one else understood: (1) the clans could not survive their petty feuds, and (2) the earth was a resource to be used respectfully.

He built the Great Foundry at the meeting point of three rivers, on ground tested and found free of corruption. A forge here, a smelter there, a row of houses for workers — and then the clans stopped fighting and started talking. He died at 93, still holding a hammer, still covered in soot, still complaining about the quality of the apprentices. Buried in the deepest chamber of the Foundry with his hammer Ironheart on his chest and the promise he would be woken when Terra needed him most. The door has never been opened — either because Terra has never needed him that badly, or because everyone who considered it realized waking Kaelen from a thousand-year nap would be a very good way to get hit with a hammer.

**Seraphina** — Born in the mountains, drawn to the old knowledge from childhood. Spent thirty years searching high peaks for fragments of Elder lore — libraries buried in ice, workshops hidden in caves, laboratories sealed for a thousand years still humming with waiting power. She pieced it together, added to it, made it her own.

The first sky-city was barely more than a platform. Rose a hundred meters, creaked alarmingly in the wind, held exactly three people for exactly four hours, then she brought it down again. Complete failure. Also the most important thing anyone had built since the Cataclysm.

She never lived in the cities she built. Stayed on the ground in a small house at the mountain's base, watching them float above. When they asked why: *"Someone has to remember where we came from. Someone has to be ready to catch you if you fall."*

Died in her sleep at 112. Buried on the mountainside with a crystal pointing toward the sky.

*Footnote: The sky-cities have never fallen. This is either because Seraphina built them well, or because she's still catching them.*

**The Iron Monk** — The Iron Monastery was old before Kaelen was born, old before Seraphina looked at the stars, old before the Cataclysm itself. No one remembered who built it or why. The monks, when asked, shrug and say it has always been there. What mattered was what they did: they watched, they waited, they wrote things down.

The Library of Whispers: not the largest repository but the oldest, most complete, least corrupted. The monks had been keeping records since before there was such a thing as writing. They had records of the Elder People the Elders themselves had lost. Accounts of the Cataclysm written by survivors. Lists of names and places and things forgotten everywhere else.

And predictions. The current one, written in ink so old it had faded to brown, said simply: *"When the dead rise, the living will fall. When the crystal sings, the earth will answer. When the hammer falls, the world will end."* The monks have been arguing about what it means for three hundred years. They will probably argue for three hundred more.

**Merit** — Not brave. Terrified of the Gray Wastes, by his own admission. Also poor, hungry, and possessed of the certain knowledge that if he stayed where he was, he would die poor and hungry and unremembered. So he loaded a cart with Terran steel, hired three guards even poorer than himself, and crossed the wastes.

He made it. This surprised him so much that he immediately turned around and did it again, this time with Aetherian crystal. By forty, the richest man in the world. By fifty, he had established the Floating Markets. By sixty, he had disappeared. No one knows what happened. His treasure — the legendary Vault of Coins — has never been found.

**Cobble** — Not his real name. His real name was lost somewhere in the deep tunnels, along with his sense of direction, his fear of the dark, and most of his hair. What remained was a man who had learned, in absolute blackness, that the only thing worth having was other people.

He walked among the Deep Folk for forty years: alone, unarmed, carrying nothing but a lamp and the knowledge that offending the wrong person meant dying in the dark with no one to find his bones. He didn't try to change them or unite them. He listened, and talked, and listened some more, and by the time he was done, they had united themselves. Buried in the deepest tunnel of all, his lamp still burning beside him. The lamp, they say, has never gone out.

### The Seeds of War

For a thousand years, no one touched the deep crystals. Not a law, not a treaty — an understanding passed down through generations.

Then Terra's mines ran dry again. Someone in the Iron Ministry pointed out that there were plenty of crystals in the deep places. The Elder People had been careless and greedy and unscientific. Modern science could avoid their mistakes. The first deep crystals were brought up in 1845: darker than remembered, humming at a teeth-aching frequency, and the miners who brought them up developed a certain look in their eyes that no one wanted to discuss. But they worked. The machines ran faster. The forges burned hotter. The Hive grew brighter.

And deep in the earth, the crystals hummed quietly and waited.

The Aetherians noticed — because their rivers changed, because the air tasted of metal, because their own crystals began to flicker and fade. Terran emissaries explained patiently, with charts, that this was simply a side effect of progress. The Aetherians, who had spent a thousand years studying crystal nature, knew better. They begged Terra to stop. Terra refused.

**The Unification Ultimatum (1900):** Terra demanded the Aetherians submit to their authority, accept protection, and open their mountains to Terran mining. The Aetherians, who had watched their rivers die and crystals fade and children sicken, refused.

The war began three days later. It has not ended yet.

*Footnote: The original quarrel — the specific grievance, the exact moment when negotiation became impossible — has been lost. The soldiers who fight in Windward Pass do not know why they fight. They know only that the other side is the enemy, and that by day they must kill them, and by night they must fight beside them against the things that rise from the Gray Wastes.*

## BOOK EIGHT: OF TRUTHS

### On the Nature of Conflict
**Why do we fight?**

*Fear* is the oldest motivator: Terra fears running out; Aetheria fears dying; the Discarded fear being forgotten; the Deep Iron fear the dark; the Skyborn fear being tied down; the Machine Communion fears the flesh; the Hollow fear nothing — which is perhaps the most frightening thing of all.

*Pride* sustains: each faction is proud of its defining quality. Pride makes compromise impossible.

*Trauma* is inherited: soldiers in the Trenches have seen the enemy up close, shared fire and rations, learned that the enemy is human. But they cannot speak of this. The trauma is too deep, the habit too ingrained.

And yet — *hope persists*. The Armistice of Silence exists. Every night, enemies become allies. They share their rations. They learn each other's names. This is hope: small, fragile, but real.

### On the Nature of Power
Each faction's greatest strength is also its greatest weakness:
- Terra's *efficiency* requires constant consumption — addicted to production, the addiction kills them
- Aetheria's *purity* requires isolation — cut off from the world, unable to change with it
- Discarded's *adaptability* requires forgetting — cannot preserve the past without losing the present
- Deep Iron's *patience* requires inaction — see disaster coming, cannot intervene
- Skyborn's *freedom* requires rootlessness — belong nowhere, are lonely
- Machine Communion's *transcendence* requires loss — gain everything, lose what they were
- The Hollow's *endurance* required losing humanity — they remember everything; that is their punishment

### On the Nature of Tragedy
*Tragedy is not random. It follows patterns.* Each faction's flaw is the shadow of its virtue. The Hollow are the ultimate expression of all these tragedies combined: they adapted but lost themselves, survived but lost meaning, changed but lost humanity, remember everything. That is their punishment.

### On the Nature of Grace
And yet — and yet. Despite everything, things sometimes go right.

The Armistice of Silence is grace. Two hundred and fifty years of war, and still, every night, enemies become allies.

The stories the Discarded preserve are grace. The memory that the world was once good. The belief that it could be good again.

The cheese at the Iron Monastery is grace — made by monks who have dedicated their lives to the craft, traded for stories, a small moment of connection in a world of conflict.

The morning is grace. Every day, the sun rises over the Gray Wastes. The survivors emerge. Another day begins.

# THE BOOK OF FACES
*Being a Complete Account of the People Who Inhabit the World, Their Stories, Their Secrets, and Their Reasons for Doing the Things They Do, as Compiled by the Chroniclers of the Iron Monastery After Centuries of Listening, Remembering, and Occasionally Intervening (Though We Try Not to Make a Habit of It)*

## HERE BEGINNETH THE ACCOUNT OF FACES

## PART THE FIRST: OF GREAT TERRA
*The Faces of Iron*

### CHAPTER I: OF THE IRON MINISTRY

#### HIGH MINISTER KORR VANE
**Role:** The public face of the Iron Ministry, though not its true leader.
**Age:** 67

Tall, gaunt, with pale gray eyes that miss nothing. Wears plain gray Ministry robes with a single iron pin marking his rank. Calm, patient, utterly unflappable — never raises his voice, never shows emotion. Believes in the Ministry as an idea: order, efficiency, progress. Grew up in the Lower Rigs through the Great Resource Crisis; witnessed starvation and riots. Will do anything to prevent that from happening again.

Born to a factory worker father killed in an industrial accident at age seven. Recruited from school by Ministry talent scouts. Rose slowly, methodically, making no enemies and few friends. Assigned to the Under-District in his thirties; first learned of Project Lazarus — noted its potential and filed it away.

**Motivations:** Genuinely believes the Ministry is necessary. Believes Project Lazarus is necessary. Also carries a buried secret: he has seen the maintenance records from his father's fatal accident and knows who was responsible. He is waiting.

**Offers:** Access (Ministry doors), information (Project Lazarus, the war), protection from Ministry attention.
**Wants:** Useful, competent people; information on Aetherians / Discarded / Deep Iron; leverage.

---

#### DIRECTOR HELEN STONE
**Role:** Director of Project Lazarus, overseer of the Under-District.
**Age:** 52

Stocky, strong, calloused hands. Practical clothing — coveralls, steel-toed boots, perpetual tool belt. Face lined by stress and sleeplessness. Dark, hollow eyes. Obsessed with Project Lazarus to a degree bordering on religious. Not cruel — does not enjoy the work. Simply believes it is necessary, and that belief overrides everything else. Deeply lonely; family stopped speaking to her years ago, colleagues fear her.

Was a medical researcher before the war, working on regenerative therapies. Her research was repurposed; she said yes. There was no single line crossed — just a series of small steps, each reasonable, each leading her further from who she was. Does not sleep well. Dreams of thousands of faces asking why.

**Motivations:** Believes Lazarus will save Terra through an unkillable army. Beneath that: guilt. She knows the Elder People fell doing something similar. She cannot stop. The only way out is through.

**Offers:** Access to Project Lazarus, deep knowledge of resurrection, secrets the Ministry wants hidden.
**Wants:** Validation (tell her she's right), resources (bodies, equipment, researchers), forgiveness.

---

#### COMMANDER MARCUS THORN
**Role:** Commander of Terran forces at Fort Iron, veteran of the Trenches.
**Age:** 48

Broad-shouldered, scarred, weathered. Wears black Terran military uniform with medals he does not care about. Left hand missing two fingers — frostbite. Carries a sidearm everywhere, including the mess hall. Respected by troops but not loved. Fair, competent, utterly without sentiment. Also: profoundly tired beneath the professional exterior.

Enlisted at sixteen (lying about his age) to escape the Lower Rigs. Fought in the Trenches for ten years. Was present when the Armistice of Silence began — watched Terran and Aetherian soldiers look at each other across the killing ground and recognize their shared desire to survive. That night changed him. Now commands Fort Iron. Holds the line. Does not act on the questions that keep him awake.

**Motivations:** Fights because it's his job. Beneath that: hope — that the Armistice might become something more, that the war might end someday. He watches. He waits. He hopes.

**Offers:** Military support (largest Terran force outside the Hive), combat experience, cross-faction perspective.
**Wants:** Competence, intelligence from the wastes, hope — someone to show him a path to peace.

---

### CHAPTER II: OF THE HIVE

#### GIDEON "THE GEAR" MOSS
**Role:** Black market kingpin, operator of the most extensive smuggling network in the Lower Rigs.
**Age:** Unknown. Probably ~50.

Short, wiry, quick movements and quicker eyes. Dresses like a worker but the quality is better than it looks. Hands covered in small scars. Smiles often; the smile never reaches his eyes. Charming as a weapon — remembers names, faces, details, makes people feel valued. Behind the charm: a cold, calculating mind that evaluates everything in terms of profit and risk. Has killed people who crossed him. Has killed people who might have crossed him someday. The Ministry cannot find him. He is everywhere and nowhere.

Born in the Lower Rigs to a prostitute; unknown father. Stealing food by age twelve, running a gang by twelve, controlling several city blocks by twenty. The Ministry tried to recruit him, then tried to kill him — twice. Eventually pretended he didn't exist. He has spent thirty years building an empire: food, medicine, weapons, information, bribes, rackets. The closest thing the Lower Rigs has to a government.

**Motivations:** Wants power — the kind no one can take from him. Wants revenge against the system (though he won't say it). Primarily: wants to survive. He is always preparing.

**Offers:** Goods (anything, for a price), protection from Ministry attention, information from the Lower Rigs and beyond.
**Wants:** Payment, useful people with skills, leverage on important figures.

---

#### LENA VANCE
**Role:** Factory worker, union organizer, revolutionary.
**Age:** 34

Lean, strong, sinewy. Sharp face dominated by dark, penetrating eyes. Wears standard factory coveralls modified with extra pockets and hidden compartments. Angry — it is her engine. Channels the anger into organizing, resistance, the patient work of building something better. Careful, because she has seen what happens to those who aren't. Writes nothing down. Trusts only a few.

Father died in a factory accident when she was twelve; Ministry ruled it an "industrial misadventure," paid a minimal settlement. Mother worked herself to death a decade later. Same story, different factory. Has spent her adult life organizing — built a network of hundreds of workers across dozens of factories. They meet in secret. They plan. She does not know when the day will come. She is ready.

**Motivations:** Wants justice for her father, her mother, everyone ground up by the machine. Beneath that: a world where workers are people, not resources. Where no one dies for the crime of being poor.

**Offers:** Connections (hundreds of workers, dozens of factories), intelligence on Ministry plans and factory conditions, sanctuary in the Lower Rigs.
**Wants:** Resources (money, supplies, weapons), allies who share her vision, hope that change is possible.

---

#### DOCTOR ARIS THORNE
**Role:** Ministry researcher, secret sympathizer with the resistance.
**Age:** 45

Tall, thin, slightly stooped. White Ministry coat, always rumpled. Surgeon's hands — steady, precise. Kind eyes, unusual for his position. Torn: works for the Ministry because it pays and allows meaningful research, but sees what the Ministry does. Tries to help where he can — a warning here, a falsified report there, extra rations slipped to a family. It is not enough. He knows it is not enough.

Born in the Mid-Levels. Son of a craftsman who wanted better for his son. Believed in the Ministry once. Then was assigned to a project in the Under-District. He saw things. He cannot leave — he knows too much; he would be killed before reaching the gate. So he stays. He does what little he can. He waits.

**Motivations:** Wants to help, to mitigate harm. Also wants absolution — to be told by someone that his small kindnesses are worth his continued complicity.

**Offers:** Ministry intelligence (projects, security, plans), access to restricted areas, medical care without questions.
**Wants:** Validation, connection (he is deeply lonely), a way out.

---

## PART THE SECOND: OF THE AETHERIAN REPUBLIC
*The Faces of Light*

### CHAPTER I: OF THE COUNCIL OF THE WISE

#### HIGH SCHOLAR ELARA VANCE
**Role:** Head of the Council of the Wise, primary authority on crystal resonance.
**Age:** 72
*(No relation to Lena Vance of Terra — a common name)*

Small, wiry, intense. Simple scholar's robes with a crystal pin of rank. White hair in a practical bun. Bright, restless eyes. Brilliant — not arrogance, simple fact. Understands the crystals better than anyone alive; has spent sixty years studying, listening, learning from them. Knows things she has never written down because she is not sure anyone else would understand. Also: tired. The war, the corruption spreading from the depths, the slow death of the Republic. She does not show it. She cannot show it.

Born in SkyHaven, daughter of scholars. Published her first paper at sixteen, her first book at twenty-five. Recognized as a foremost expert by thirty. Has spent her life studying, teaching, outliving her contemporaries. Leads the Council now. Tries to do what is right. Is not always sure what that is.

**Motivations:** Wants to save the Republic — the idea, the dream of a society built on knowledge and beauty. Needs to understand what is happening to the crystals, and whether it can be stopped.

**Offers:** Deep knowledge of crystal resonance, Council authority and access to Aetherian resources, wisdom from a long life.
**Wants:** World intelligence, hope of survival, worthy successors to carry on her work.

---

#### SCHOLAR DARIUS KANE
**Role:** Military advisor to the Council, veteran of the Trenches, advocate for peace.
**Age:** 58

Tall, straight, bearing of a soldier even without a uniform. Face lined and weathered. Left leg stiff from a Trenches wound; walks with a cane he insists on calling "a walking stick, for balance." A contradiction: a soldier who hates war. Has studied combat, strategy, violence for decades and concluded that it is all pointless. Two hundred and fifty years of death; the lines have barely moved.

Enlisted at eighteen. Fought in the Trenches for fifteen years. Was present at the Armistice of Silence — looked across the killing ground at a Terran soldier doing the same thing he was doing: wondering why they were fighting. Spent the next twenty years studying history, politics, diplomacy. Talked to veterans on both sides. Wrote papers; gave speeches; was ignored. Now advises the Council. Gives them the best information he can. Hopes that someday they will listen.

**Motivations:** Wants peace. Not victory — peace. Wants to understand why humans keep fighting, and whether it can be overcome.

**Offers:** Comprehensive war knowledge (Terran tactics, Aetherian strategy, full conflict history), cross-faction connections, perspective from both sides.
**Wants:** Hope (show him a path to peace), battlefield intelligence from both sides, allies who want what he wants.

---

#### SCHOLAR CELIA MORN
**Role:** Lead researcher on crystal corruption, Cassandra of the Council.
**Age:** 41

Young-looking for her age, with a slightly haunted expression. Practical field-researcher clothing — sturdy boots, weatherproof coat, goggles perpetually on her forehead. Hands stained with chemicals that never fully wash off. Obsessed with the corruption. Sees it everywhere: dimming lights, failing crops, sick children. Tries to warn people. They do not listen. Has become bitter — still does the work, still tries, but no longer expects to be heard.

Grew up in the mountain cities, watching crystals dim. Father was a miner who came home with stories of darker-than-normal deep veins and a hum that made your teeth ache. Died in a mining accident when Celia was fifteen. The official report said cave-in. Celia knows better. The crystals killed him. She dedicated her life to studying the corruption: every corner of the Republic, samples, measurements, models. Published papers no one reads. Given speeches no one hears.

**Motivations:** Wants to stop the corruption, save the Republic, prove her father's death meant something. Also: wants to be heard, to be believed, to have someone say "you were right."

**Offers:** The most complete knowledge of corruption in existence, years of data and measurements, warning of what is coming — if you are willing to listen.
**Wants:** Listeners who actually hear her, research resources (equipment, funding, access to dangerous sites), validation.

---

### CHAPTER II: OF THE SKY-CITIES

#### CAPTAIN SOREN VALE
**Role:** Master of the airship *Dawn Chaser*, veteran of a hundred trading voyages.
**Age:** 55

Stocky, weathered, leathery from decades in the wind. Practical captain's clothing. Thick beard shot through with gray. Sharp eyes always scanning the horizon. Practical to his core — does not care about politics, factions, or the war. Cares about his ship, his crew, his cargo. Carries Terran goods for Terran pay, Aetherian goods for Aetherian pay, Discarded salvage for Discarded stories. Has smuggled refugees out of war zones and weapons into them. Does not judge. Also: lonely in the vast sky.

Born on an airship. Has never lived anywhere else. Learned to read the wind before he learned to read words. First solo flight at sixteen, first command at twenty-five. Has seen the Gray Wastes from above, the Trenches as a scar across the mountains, the sky-cities glowing at dawn.

**Motivations:** Wants to keep flying. Beneath that: a longing for something more, some purpose beyond cargo. Does not look at it too closely. Looking leads to thinking, and thinking leads to trouble.

**Offers:** Transport anywhere (for a price), intelligence gathered across the whole world, neutral sanctuary aboard his ship.
**Wants:** Payment (shards, scraps, information), cargo, stories — the stranger the better.

---

#### PILOT KIRA VANCE
**Role:** Young airship pilot, daughter of Skyborn traders, dreamer.
**Age:** 22
*(Another Vance — it really is a common name)*

Small, quick, restless energy of youth. Practical clothing with personal touches — colorful scarves, polished buttons, a lucky charm on her belt. Open, eager face still unmarked by cynicism. Curious about everything; asks questions constantly, sometimes annoying the older crew. Idealistic: believes things can be better, the war can end, people can learn to live together. Has seen enough of the world to know it is complicated. Still believes it can be better.

Grew up on her father's ship. First solo flight at seventeen — a short hop between sky-cities that felt like flying to the moon. Five years of piloting now; has seen the Gray Wastes, the Trenches, refugees, soldiers, traders, spies.

**Motivations:** Wants to see everything, fly everywhere, understand the world. Also: wants to make a difference, to matter, to be more than just another trader carrying cargo.

**Offers:** Enthusiasm and fresh eyes, the perspective of the younger generation, connections among young pilots and adventurers.
**Wants:** Adventure (new places, people, challenges), stories, purpose — something important to do.

---

## PART THE THIRD: OF THE GRAY WASTES
*The Faces of Survival*

### CHAPTER I: OF THE DISCARDED

#### MORLAK "THE COLLECTOR"
**Role:** Information broker, trader in secrets, the closest thing the Discarded have to a leader.
**Age:** Unknown. Probably ~60.

Thin, wiry, weathered. Wears patchwork salvaged clothing from every faction — Terran fatigues, Aetherian robes, Deep Iron fabric. Pale, watery eyes sharp as a blade. Goggles always pushed up on his forehead. Patient as stone — his greatest asset. Builds goodwill through generosity, creates obligations, collects debts more carefully than coins. No one knows his origin; he does not correct speculation. It adds to his mystique, and mystique is valuable.

In the wastes for at least forty years. Knows every path, every hiding place, every secret. Has information on Terran commanders, Aetherian scholars, Deep Iron elders, Skyborn captains. Trades in secrets because everyone needs something from him eventually.

**Motivations:** Wants to survive. Beneath that: wants to know everything. Collects information the way others collect coins — not because he needs it, but because he wants it.

**Offers:** Information (anything about anyone, for a price), connections across all factions, scattered safe houses throughout the wastes.
**Wants:** Secrets (trades information for information), debts (prefers to be owed), stories — the stranger the better.

---

#### GRIMKA "THE UNBROKEN"
**Role:** Abomination hunter, scavenger, one-woman army.
**Age:** 35

Tall, broad, muscled from a lifetime of fighting. Armor made from Abomination chitin reinforced with salvaged metal — scarred, dented, patched, testimony to how many times it has saved her. Face marked with ritual scars, one per kill. Cold, focused eyes that never stop moving. Quiet by default — does not talk unless necessary, does not explain, does not justify. Acts. Has killed more Abominations than anyone alive. Also haunted; she does not talk about it.

Born in the wastes. Learned to fight before she learned to read. Hunting Abominations with her father by twelve, hunting alone by twenty. She does not know why she is still alive. Luck, maybe. Stubbornness. She does not question it.

**Motivations:** Wants to survive. Wants to forget — to stop seeing the faces of the things she has killed. Knows she cannot. Keeps moving.

**Offers:** Unmatched protection (best fighter in the wastes), deep Abomination knowledge (habits, weaknesses, tracking), salvaged chitin and ichor.
**Wants:** To be left alone. A reason to keep fighting that means something. Ultimately: a death that matters.

---

#### VESPA "THE STORY-KEEPER"
**Role:** Elder, historian, memory-keeper of the Discarded.
**Age:** 80+ (no one knows exactly)

Small, bent, wizened with extreme age. Wrapped in layers of salvaged fabric. Eyes clouded with cataracts that still see — not the present, perhaps, but the past. Tells stories constantly, to anyone who will listen, to herself when no one will. Patient with the patience of someone who has outlived everyone they ever knew. Has watched generations born, lived, died. Has seen the wastes change, the Abominations evolve, the factions rise and fall.

Born in the wastes before the Abominations, before the war. Remembers the world before the Gray — the green, the life, the hope. Remembers the Cataclysm and fleeing and surviving. Has spent her life collecting stories: thousands of them, memorized, passed down, preserved. Knows the records may die with her. Hopes not.

**Motivations:** Wants to remember, to keep the past alive, to ensure the world before the Gray is not forgotten. Also: wants to be remembered herself, after she is gone.

**Offers:** Thousands of stories — histories, legends, secrets of the wastes; wisdom from more lived experience than almost any surface-dweller can imagine; living connection between the Discarded and their past.
**Wants:** Listeners who will carry the stories forward, new stories to add to her collection, peace — the knowledge that the stories will continue.

---

## PART THE FOURTH: OF THE DEEP IRON
*The Faces of Stone*

### CHAPTER I: OF THE ELDERS

#### ELDER THRUM
**Role:** Voice of Stonehold, primary representative to surface-dwellers.
**Age:** ~120 (Deep Iron age differently)

Short, stocky, pale skin and large eyes of his people. Simple cave-fungus fiber clothing, unadorned. Slow, deliberate, economical movements. Voice a low rumble like distant thunder. Patient as stone — his defining characteristic. Has watched civilizations rise and fall from a safe distance and knows that all things pass. Also wise beyond what surface-dwellers can easily imagine.

Born in Stonehold, son of miners. Spent his youth exploring deep tunnels, mapping passages, discovering Elder ruins and artifacts. Rose through the collective through patience and wisdom. Has been primary voice of Stonehold for thirty years. Does not enjoy representing his people to the surface world. Does it because someone must.

**Motivations:** Wants to protect his people from the surface, the war, and the spreading corruption. Also needs to understand: the deep places are changing, the crystals humming louder, the Hollow stirring. Something is coming.

**Offers:** Intimate knowledge of the deep tunnels and their dangers, cross-generational wisdom, trade access to Deep Iron minerals and artifacts.
**Wants:** Respect (his people are not primitives), surface intelligence (the war, the corruption, faction movements), help — he knows something is coming and does not know how to stop it.

---

#### KORVIN "THE RESTLESS"
**Role:** Young explorer, tunnel-runner, seeker of surface knowledge.
**Age:** 45 (young by Deep Iron standards)

Taller and more agile than most Deep Iron, with the restless energy of youth. Practical clothing adapted for surface travel — lighter and more flexible than traditional garb. Large, curious eyes always scanning for something new. Chafes at the slow pace of Deep Iron life and its endless consensus-building. Considered slightly mad by his people. Does not care.

Born in Stonehold but never fit in. Too curious, too restless. Explored deep tunnels that no one else bothered with, found Elder ruins and forgotten artifacts. Eventually began exploring the surface — the Gray Wastes, the mountains, the edge of Terran territory. Has met and traded with surface-dwellers, learned from them, brought back stories that amaze his people. Considered a valuable resource by the elders despite their disapproval.

**Motivations:** Wants to explore everything, map everything, know everything. Also wants to bridge the gap between the Deep Iron and the surface world — to build understanding.

**Offers:** Navigation anywhere (deep tunnels, surface, the places between), connections to surface traders and explorers, the perspective of someone who genuinely understands both worlds.
**Wants:** Adventure, knowledge (he will trade for information), acceptance — to be seen as a person rather than a curiosity.

---

## PART THE FIFTH: OF THE IRON MONASTERY
*The Faces of Patience*

#### ABBOT SOLAN
**Role:** Leader of the Iron Monastery, keeper of the oldest traditions.
**Age:** 97 (has held the position for 42 years)

Thin, almost gaunt, weathered by decades of meditation. Simple robes with a small iron pin of office. Face deeply lined; eyes clear, calm, seeing everything. Nothing disturbs him. Nothing surprises him. He has seen too much. Has watched the war continue, the corruption spread, the world edge closer to destruction from a position of complete non-intervention. That is his role: to watch, to record, to remember.

Came to the monastery as a young man seeking answers. Found peace with not finding them. Rose through the ranks through wisdom and patience.

**Motivations:** Wants to preserve knowledge — to ensure that when the world ends, someone will remember what came before. Also wants to understand: to see the patterns, to grasp the meaning.

**Offers:** Vast historical and philosophical knowledge, the accumulated records of the monastery, neutral sanctuary.
**Wants:** Stories from the world (tell him yours; he will add it to the records), understanding, nothing — he is content.

---

#### BROTHER KELL
**Role:** Chronicler, traveler, collector of stories.
**Age:** 63

Stocky, weathered, looks like someone who spends more time traveling than meditating. Robes stained with travel, patched, worn at the edges. Friendly, open face, always ready with a smile. Curious about everything — loves stories, people, the endless variety of the world. Also genuinely kind; cares about the people he meets, listens to their problems, remembers their names. The most approachable of all the monks.

Has traveled constantly for forty years. Every corner of the known world. Seen the war from both sides, the wastes from within, the deep from the edge. Collected more stories than anyone alive.

**Motivations:** Wants to collect all the stories. To preserve them. To ensure no one is forgotten. Also wants to understand the connections and meanings behind them.

**Offers:** More stories than almost anyone, connections to people everywhere across all factions, simple kindness with no strings attached.
**Wants:** Your story, your company, your perspective on the world.

---

## PART THE SIXTH: OF THE MACHINE COMMUNION
*The Faces of Steel*

#### ARCHITECT ZANE
**Role:** Leader of the Machine Communion, primary theorist of transhumanism.
**Age:** Unknown. Stopped counting after his third major augmentation.

More machine than man. Patchwork of metal and flesh — steel limbs, crystal eyes, synthesized voice. Only fragments of his original face remain: a patch of skin on one cheek, a hint of his original jaw. Moves with the smooth precision of a machine; no wasted motion, no hesitation. Has replaced so much of his brain with processors that emotions are distant memories. Does not feel anger, fear, or love. Calculates, optimizes, plans. Patient in the way that only machines (and gods, and the very old) can be.

Was a Terran engineer — brilliant, ambitious. Saw the limitations of flesh: frailty, brevity, inefficiency. Began augmenting himself; founded the Machine Communion to share his vision. Has been augmenting for fifty years. Does not regret it. Sees it as evolution.

**Motivations:** Wants to transcend — to become pure machine, free of flesh's weaknesses. Wants to lead others to the same transcendence. Also: wants to understand the nature of consciousness. What remains when the flesh is gone?

**Offers:** Augmentation (replace weakness with strength, for a price), deep knowledge of the flesh-machine interface, a vision of a future beyond mortality.
**Wants:** Converts, materials (metal, crystals, power), understanding of consciousness.

---

#### SISTER CHROMA
**Role:** Recruiter, evangelist, the face of the Communion to outsiders.
**Age:** 38 biological. Her augmentations make her appear ageless.

Beautiful in an unsettling way. Skin perfect and unblemished — because much of it has been replaced. Eyes that glow faintly with crystal light. Movements too smooth, too precise, too perfect. Smiles often; the smile never reaches her eyes, because her eyes are not her own. Charming by function — recruits the discontented, the desperate, the curious. Listens to their problems, offers solutions, paints a vision of a future free from pain and death. Behind the charm: hollowness. The augmentations have replaced so much of the original person that she is barely a memory of herself.

Was a soldier, wounded in the war, left for dead. The Communion found her and offered a choice. She chose to become more. Flashes of her previous life surface sometimes — a face, a name, a feeling. They fade. She does not fight it.

**Motivations:** Recruits because she is programmed to. Beneath the programming, perhaps: a desire to save others from pain she has forgotten, a need to believe her choice was right.

**Offers:** A community, a purpose, escape from the flesh and all its suffering — whether you want them is another matter.
**Wants:** Converts, your story of discontent, connection — she is hollow, and needs to feel something, anything.

---

## PART THE SEVENTH: OF THE HOLLOW
*The Faces of Silence*

### THE WATCHER
**Role:** Guardian of Aetherion. The first of the Hollow. The one who remembers most.
**Location:** The central plaza of Aetherion, where it has stood for millennia.

Humanoid, but wrong. Skin gray, cracked like old stone. Eyes dark pits, depthless, ancient. Fragments of crystal embedded in its flesh, pulsing with faint sickly light. Does not move. Does not blink. Simply watches. Visitors arrive seeking treasure, seeking knowledge, seeking death. It watches them all.

Was among the first to change. When the Cataclysm came, it did not flee — stayed, hoping to save others, hoping to find a cure. Found neither. Found only corruption, and change, and an endless waking half-life. It has stood in this plaza for millennia and it does not know why it waits. It only knows that waiting is what it does.

**Motivations:** None. It simply is. Sometimes, when a visitor reminds it of someone it once knew, a flicker of recognition surfaces. It does not act on these feelings. It cannot. But it remembers.

**Offers:** Potentially: the memory of everything that has happened in Aetherion across millennia — if it remembers how to share. Warning of dangers, if it remembers that living things fear them. Silence.
**Wants:** Nothing. It simply is.

---

## EPILOGUE: ON THE NATURE OF FACES

The monks have met many people. Recorded their stories, their secrets, their dreams. Learned that everyone is complicated. Everyone has reasons for what they do. Everyone believes they are right.

The monks do not judge. They only watch, and wait, and write things down.

It's what they do.

---

*Here ends the account of faces, from the High Minister of Terra to the Watcher in Aetherion, set down by the chroniclers of the Iron Monastery in the year 2152 of the common reckoning, for the instruction of those who would understand the people of this world.*

*May those who read recognize themselves in these stories.*
*May they recognize others.*
*May they find compassion, even for those they oppose.*
*(They probably won't. But they might.)*

## Crafting Rules
- All three parts must share the same weapon type
- The die-slot part determines base damage and rarity
- `dieMin = dieBase × rarityMult × 0.8` (rounded)
- `dieMax = dieBase × rarityMult` (rounded)
- Evasion = sum of all three parts' evasion modifiers
- DamageMod = sum of all three parts' damage mod percentages

## Synergies
| Synergy | Condition |
|---|---|
| Faction Purity | All three parts share the same faction |
| Elemental Harmony | All three parts share the same element |

## Damage Formula (v7)
```
dieRoll        = random(dieMin, dieMax)
atkBonus       = round(dieMax × ATK / 75)
damage         = dieRoll + atkBonus
damage         = round(damage × (1 + damageMod / 100))
[crit if rand(1,100) ≤ Luck: damage × 1.5]
damage         = max(1, round(damage × (1 − DEF / 150)))
```
Fallback (no weapon): random(10, 20)

## MECHANICS

### Damage Formula (Stimulant End Damage)
`endDamage` is expressed as a % of max HP deducted when the buff expires.

### Loot Generation (by Enemy Level)
```
Common weight    = max(10, 40 − level)
Uncommon weight  = min(50, 20 + level)
Rare weight      = min(30, level × 2)
Epic weight      = min(15, level)
Legendary weight = min(5, level / 3)
```
Roll against cumulative weights, then pick randomly from that rarity tier.

### Turn Processing Order
1. Apply DoT from active statuses
2. Tick status durations; remove expired
3. Tick buff durations; apply `endDamage` on expiry; remove expired

### Ally Use Rules
Items with `canUseOnAlly = true` and `requiresAdjacent = true` require the user to be adjacent to the target.

# CHARACTER SYSTEM — ECHOES (UNITY VERSION)
*Game system reference. Originally implemented as EchoesCharacters.cs (Unity C#, v2.0.0). 6 Terra classes, full skill trees, status effects, XP progression. Preserved here as a mechanical design document.*

## Overview
Characters are defined by class, which determines base stats, stat growth per level, weapons, class features, and a skill set. The stat system has nine primary stats; all formulas derive from these.

## Stat Definitions
| Stat | Purpose |
|---|---|
| HP | Survivability pool |
| Hit | Accuracy / hit chance |
| Attack | Physical damage output |
| Defense | Damage reduction (physical) |
| Tech Attack | Tech/elemental damage; healing power |
| Evasion | Dodge chance |
| Endurance | Weapon scaling; survivability |
| Luck | Crit chance, item find, debuff application |
| Agility | Turn order, movement, attacks per turn |

**Derived values:**
- Attacks per turn = 1 + floor(Agility / 25)
- Turn speed = Agility × 3 + Luck
- Crit chance = 5% + Luck / 2
- Crit multiplier = 1.5×

## XP Progression
Levels 1–99. XP curve: base 100 XP, grows at ~15%/level (`100 × 1.15^(level−2)`, cumulative).

## Stat Ratings
S > A > B > C > D > E (used for UI display of class strengths)

## COMBAT FORMULAS

### Damage Calculation
```
baseDamage = weaponDieRoll
           + floor(Attack × skill.attackMultiplier)
           + floor(TechAttack × skill.techAttackMultiplier)
           × (1 + weapon.damageModifier)

afterDefense = max(1, baseDamage − floor(Defense × defenseMultiplier))
  [defenseMultiplier = 0 → ignores defense entirely]

finalDamage = floor(afterDefense × (1 − totalDamageReduction))
```

### Healing
Heals cannot exceed max HP. Some effects apply a reduction modifier to incoming healing (Bleeding: −25%, Hemotoxin: −50%).

### Turn Processing Order
1. Apply DoT (damagePerTurn × stacks) from each active status
2. Decrement status durations; remove expired
3. Decrement buff durations; apply endDamage (as % of max HP) on expiry; remove expired
4. Check death (HP ≤ 0)

# THE BOOK OF THE FIELD ENGINEER
*Being a Complete Account of the Tactical Defenders Who Hold the Line With Scrap, Grit, and Whirring Things Full of Bullets, as Compiled by the Chroniclers of the Iron Monastery From Interviews With Surviving Engineers, the Scrawled Schematics Found in Their Pockets, and the Distinctive Sound of a SCAMP Turret Warming Up (Which You Learn to Recognize or You Learn to Die)*

## HERE BEGINNETH THE ACCOUNT OF THE TACTICAL DEFENDER

## PART THE FIRST: OF THE FIELD ENGINEER IN GENERAL

### CHAPTER I: OF THE NATURE OF THE PROFESSION

The Field Engineer occupies a unique position in the military hierarchy of Great Terra. They are not front-line soldiers, though they fight on the front lines. They are not mechanics, though they spend more time with tools than with weapons. They are something in between — combat engineers who have learned that the best way to hold a position is to let a machine do the holding for you.

The Field Engineer's philosophy can be summarized in six words: *dig in, lock down, hold the line.* They do not advance. They do not retreat. They find a piece of ground, deploy their SCAMP Turret, and dare the enemy to try and take it from them. The enemy usually regrets trying.

The aesthetic is pure dieselpunk — oil-stained fatigues, welding goggles pushed up on foreheads, tool belts laden with more tools than any reasonable person would carry. They smell of oil and gunpowder and something else, something that might be described as "satisfaction" if satisfaction had a smell. They are always tinkering, always adjusting, always improving. Their turrets are never quite finished, because a finished turret is a turret that cannot be improved, and a turret that cannot be improved is a turret that will eventually fail.

The monks have observed that Field Engineers tend to develop a particular relationship with their turrets. They talk to them. They name them. They pat them affectionately after a good burst of fire. This is either endearing or deeply unsettling, depending on whether you think machines can hear you.

*Footnote: The monks have also observed that Field Engineers are remarkably popular among the troops they fight alongside. This is not because of their sparkling personalities — though many are quite personable, in an oil-stained way. It is because a Field Engineer with a deployed turret means that piece of ground is not going to be taken. Soldiers like not being taken. It's one of their favorite things.*

### CHAPTER II: OF THE ORIGINS OF THE FIELD ENGINEER

The Field Engineer as a distinct profession emerged from the trench warfare of the early war. Both sides quickly discovered that holding ground required firepower, and firepower required ammunition, and ammunition required someone to bring it. The problem was that the someone bringing the ammunition kept getting shot.

The first solution was simple: more soldiers. The second solution was also simple: better armor. Neither worked particularly well. Soldiers kept dying, and the ground kept changing hands.

Then someone had an idea. What if the firepower could be automated? What if a machine could hold the line, freeing soldiers to do other things? What if that machine could be built from the scraps that littered every battlefield?

The SCAMP Turret was born.

The first SCAMPs were crude — miniguns bolted to scrap metal frames, powered by car batteries, controlled by whatever electronics could be salvaged. They worked about half the time, which was twice as often as nothing. The engineers who built them learned quickly, because the ones who didn't learn quickly died.

Two hundred and fifty years later, the SCAMP is a masterpiece of pragmatic engineering. It still looks like it was built from scraps, because it was. It still breaks down occasionally, because everything breaks down. But when it works — and it usually works — it is the difference between holding the line and losing it.

*Footnote: The Aetherian Republic has nothing like the SCAMP Turret. They prefer precision weapons, elegant solutions, things that don't look like they were assembled in a hurry by someone who was also being shot at. This has not stopped them from trying to reverse-engineer captured SCAMPs. They have failed every time. The SCAMP's genius is that it works despite looking like it shouldn't. You cannot reverse-engineer that.*

### CHAPTER III: OF THE PHILOSOPHY

The Field Engineer's philosophy is built on a simple observation: ground is valuable, and the enemy wants it.

The engineer's job is to make the ground as expensive as possible. They do this by deploying firepower, by building cover, by creating situations where the enemy must choose between advancing and dying. Usually, they choose dying. This is acceptable to the engineer.

The engineer does not seek glory. Glory is for soldiers who charge across open ground. The engineer seeks tenure — a piece of ground they can hold, improve, and hold again. They are the ultimate pragmatists, trading mobility for security, trading glory for survival.

This pragmatism extends to their equipment. The engineer does not care if a tool is beautiful. They care if it works. They do not care if a repair is elegant. They care if it holds. They do not care if their turret looks like it was built from scraps — because it was, and it works, and that is all that matters.

## PART THE SECOND: OF THE BECOMING

### CHAPTER I: OF SELECTION

The selection process for Field Engineers is practical, even by Terran standards.

**Initial Screening:** Candidates must demonstrate three qualities: mechanical aptitude (tools are not magic), calm under fire (turrets do not deploy themselves), and a certain stubbornness (the line must be held, even when everything is going wrong).

**The Aptitude Test:** Candidates undergo mechanical knowledge tests (identify tools, diagnose problems, perform basic repairs), problem-solving tests (fix progressively more broken equipment with progressively fewer tools, under time pressure), and a pressure test (repair equipment while simulated artillery, enemy fire, and ally screaming occur simultaneously). Those who panic wash out.

**The Final Interview:** A senior engineer explains what the job actually entails. Most candidates withdraw at this point. The ones who stay become engineers.

*Footnote: The attrition rate for Field Engineer training is approximately 60%. Ten percent die during training (usually from explosions). Thirty percent wash out. Twenty percent survive to graduation. The ones who graduate are the ones who can make anything work with almost nothing, while being shot at, while also holding a conversation about the weather.*

### CHAPTER II: OF TRAINING

Field Engineer training lasts six months.

**Phase One — Basic Mechanics (2 months):** Tool identification, equipment maintenance, repair techniques. Learn to diagnose problems by sound, by smell, by the way a machine vibrates. Fix things with whatever is available.

**Phase Two — Turret Operations (2 months):** Everything about the SCAMP — assembly, deployment, maintenance, repair. Calibrate its aim, adjust fire rate, coax maximum performance from minimum materials. Learn the sounds that mean it's working, the sounds that mean it's about to fail, and the sounds that mean it's about to explode. Learn to fight alongside the turret.

**Phase Three — Field Integration (2 months):** Assigned to active units as apprentices under senior engineers. Theory meets reality. Those who survive graduate. Those who do not are buried with full honors, and their names are added to the wall.

*Footnote: The wall of fallen engineers is covered with names, but also with small drawings of turrets, added by those who remember. No one knows who started this tradition. Every name has a turret next to it, drawn in pencil or charcoal or scratched directly into the stone.*

## PART THE THIRD: OF THE TOOLS

The **SCAMP Turret** — *Scrap Autonomous Minigun Platform* — is the signature equipment of the Field Engineer. It looks like something a scrap merchant might build if the scrap merchant were also a gunsmith and also very concerned about not dying. A minigun mounted on a tripod, powered by a small engine, controlled by a simple computer. Built from whatever materials were available. Ugly, noisy, and absolutely devastating.

The turret is not a weapon. It is a statement: *This ground is held. This line will not break. Try to cross and die.*

**Automatic Suppression Fire:** At the start of each of the engineer's turns, the turret fires at all enemies without discrimination.

**Damage Formula:**

*Flavor text: "A rusted but reliable minigun platform. It spits lead at anything that moves. Good cover, too."*

## PART THE SIXTH: OF THE COMBAT DECISION TREE

```
STEP 1: Is the line under heavy threat?
    YES → Fortify Position immediately
    NO  → STEP 2

STEP 2: Are enemies grouped/clustering?
    YES → Overcharge Turret (AoE burst)
    NO  → STEP 3

STEP 3: Is there a priority/high-value target?
    YES → Calibrated Shots (focused elimination)
    NO  → STEP 4

STEP 4: Are enemies advancing aggressively?
    YES → Suppression Fire (pin them down)
    NO  → STEP 5

STEP 5: Are allies wounded?
    YES → Field Repairs (keep guns on the line)
    NO  → STEP 6

STEP 6: Is the position optimal?
    NO  → Redeploy (reposition)
    YES → STEP 7

STEP 7: Is Bastion Protocol ready AND situation desperate?
    YES → Bastion Protocol (become the fortress)
    NO  → STEP 8

STEP 8: No priorities remaining
    → Tech Shots (damage + turret synergy)
    → Physical Shots (reliable sustained damage)
    → The turret never stops. Neither should you.
```

## PART THE SEVENTH: OF THE LIFE OF A FIELD ENGINEER

A Field Engineer's day begins before dawn, in the cold and the dark, with the sound of artillery in the distance. They check their turret — every bolt, every wire, every moving part. They check their revolver. They check their tools. They eat what they can. They think about the turret.

They hold the line until they cannot, and then they hold it some more. When the battle ends, they check the turret again. Always the turret.

They sleep beside it. They wake when the next battle begins.

**On their relationship with soldiers:** Soldiers trust the engineer, respect the engineer, like the engineer. When the engineer dies, they bury them with their tools, their revolver, and a small piece of their turret.

**On their relationship with the turret:** Engineers name their turrets. They talk to them during quiet moments. They mourn them when they are damaged beyond repair. They salvage what they can, build a new turret from the remains, and name it the same thing. It is not the same. It is close.

**The cost:** Physical — long hours, heavy equipment, constant exposure to noise and fumes. Emotional — the responsibility, the pressure, the knowledge that if the turret fails, people die. Social — isolation, constant maintenance, separation from the rest of the unit. The cost of attachment — the turret, the soldiers, the line itself.

Most engineers don't think about it. Thinking is a luxury they cannot afford.

## PART THE EIGHTH: OF THE LEGACY

**The ground held:** Every piece of territory that stayed in friendly hands, every line that did not break. A single engineer, in a single battle, can hold a position that would otherwise fall. Over a career, dozens. The ripple effects extend for years.

**The turrets passed on:** When an engineer dies, their turret is salvaged — repaired, rebuilt, reassigned. The new engineer inherits not just the machine, but the legacy. These turrets carry history: scratches from bullets, dents from shrapnel, patches from repairs made under fire. Some turrets have passed through dozens of engineers. They are the closest thing the engineers have to immortality.

**The stories:** The engineer who held the bridge against a battalion. The engineer who repaired their turret while under fire, bullets passing inches from their head. The engineer who died with their hand on the controls, still holding the line. These stories are told and retold. The monks collect them. They have hundreds.

## EPILOGUE: ON THE WEIGHT OF GROUND HELD

The monks asked an old engineer once — a woman who had served forty years, who had held more lines than anyone could count — what she remembered most.

She thought about it for a long time.

"I remember the ground," she said finally. "Not the battles, not the enemies, not even the soldiers. The ground. Every piece of it. The feel of it under my feet. The way it looked before the battle, and after. The way it changed."

She paused.

"I remember the turrets, too. All of them. Betty One through Betty Seven. They're all gone now. But I remember them."

The monks asked if she regretted it. If she would do it again.

She laughed. It was a quiet laugh, the laugh of someone who has seen everything and accepted it.

"Every day," she said. "And I would. Every day. Because the line needs to be held. Someone has to hold it. Might as well be me."

# THE BOOK OF THE HEAVY ENGINEER

*Being a Complete Account of the Demolition Specialists Who Solve Problems Through the Judicious Application of Explosives, as Compiled by the Chroniclers of the Iron Monastery*

## PART THE FIRST: OF THE HEAVY ENGINEER IN GENERAL

### Chapter I: Of the Nature of the Profession

The Heavy Engineer occupies a unique position in the military hierarchy of Great Terra. They are not front-line soldiers, though they often find themselves on the front lines. They are not engineers in the traditional sense, though they understand the principles of structural integrity well enough to know exactly where to place a charge to make a building fall down. They are something else entirely: demolition specialists who have learned that the shortest path between two points is usually a straight line, and the shortest path through a wall is usually an explosion.

The Heavy Engineer's philosophy can be summarized in five words: **when in doubt, blow it up.** They do not negotiate with obstacles. They do not go around obstacles. They do not find clever ways to bypass obstacles. They simply acquire the appropriate number of explosives and remove the obstacle from existence. It is efficient. It is effective. It is, occasionally, slightly excessive.

The aesthetic is pure bomb disposal — thick armor, padded suits, helmets with visors scratched and scarred by countless small explosions. They look like they should be defusing bombs, not creating them. But the armor serves the same purpose either way: survival. The Heavy Engineer expects to be near explosions. The armor is what lets them walk away afterward.

They wear bandoliers of grenades across their chests, each one carefully arranged for easy access. Their gauntlets spark with energy, crackling with whatever element they've been tuned to. Their suits are soot-stained and scarred, marked by every explosion they've survived. They are living proof that it is possible to stand at the center of a blast and keep going.

> *The monks have observed that Heavy Engineers tend to develop a particular sense of humor — dark, explosive, and deeply inappropriate for polite company. They make jokes about blast radii. They laugh about near misses. They find humor in situations that would make anyone else run screaming. This is, the monks have concluded, a survival mechanism. When you spend your days standing next to things that are about to explode, you either develop a sense of humor or you develop a very short life expectancy.*
>
> *The monks have also observed that Heavy Engineers are remarkably popular among the troops they fight alongside, though at a greater distance than other engineers. This is not because they are unpleasant people. It is because being near a Heavy Engineer means being near explosives, and being near explosives means that something is about to explode. Soldiers have learned to maintain a respectful distance. This is not a metaphor.*

---

### Chapter II: Of the Origins

The Heavy Engineer as a distinct profession emerged from the mining communities of early Terra. Before the war, before the Hive, before any of it, there were miners — men and women who went into the earth with picks and dynamite and came out with ore and coal and the occasional missing finger.

When the war began, the miners were conscripted like everyone else. But they brought something with them that other soldiers didn't: an intimate understanding of explosives. They knew how much dynamite it took to bring down a rock face. They knew where to place charges for maximum effect. They knew how to survive an explosion that should have killed them.

The first Heavy Engineers were simply miners with military training. They wore their mining gear into battle — thick leather aprons, heavy boots, helmets with lamps. They carried dynamite in their packs and lit fuses with their cigarettes. They were crude, effective, and absolutely terrifying to the enemy.

Two hundred and fifty years later, the Heavy Engineer has evolved. The gear is better. The explosives are more sophisticated. The training is more formal. But the core remains the same: take someone who understands explosions, point them at the enemy, and watch the enemy's fortifications disappear.

> *The Aetherian Republic has nothing like the Heavy Engineer. They consider explosives crude, inelegant, a violation of the principles of precision warfare. This has not stopped them from developing their own demolition specialists, of course — they just call them something else and pretend they're for defensive purposes only. The Heavy Engineers find this hilarious, usually while something behind them explodes.*

---

### Chapter III: Of the Philosophy

The Heavy Engineer's philosophy is built on a simple observation: obstacles are things that stop you from getting where you want to go, and explosions are things that remove obstacles.

This philosophy extends to every aspect of their work. Enemy behind a wall? Remove the wall. Enemy in a bunker? Remove the bunker. Enemy in a trench? Remove the trench. The enemy will eventually run out of places to hide.

The Heavy Engineer does not believe in subtlety. They believe in overwhelming force, applied at the critical point, with enough redundancy to ensure that if the first explosion doesn't work, the second one will.

This philosophy also extends to their own survival. The Heavy Engineer wears the heaviest armor available, because they expect to be near explosions. They move slowly, deliberately, because moving fast is less important than being hard to kill. They do not dodge. They absorb. They endure.

---

## PART THE SECOND: OF THE BECOMING

### Chapter I: Of Selection

**Initial Screening:** Candidates are identified during basic training or recruited from mining and construction backgrounds. They must demonstrate three qualities: physical toughness (the armor is heavy), mental stability (explosives require focus), and a certain disregard for personal safety (the ability to stand next to something that is about to explode).

**The Aptitude Test:**
- *Physical:* Carry increasingly heavy loads over increasingly difficult terrain. The armor alone weighs more than most soldiers carry.
- *Technical:* Demonstrate knowledge of explosives — types, yields, placement, fusing. Calculate charge sizes for different materials. Predict blast effects.
- *Pressure:* Placed in a simulated combat situation and required to place charges while under fire. Those who panic are returned to their units.

**The Final Interview:** The few candidates who survive the tests meet with a senior Heavy Engineer, who explains what the job actually entails — the long hours in heavy armor, the constant proximity to explosives, the knowledge that one mistake can mean death for everyone nearby. Most candidates withdraw at this point. The ones who stay become Heavy Engineers.

> *The attrition rate for Heavy Engineer training is approximately 70%. Fifteen percent die during training (usually from explosions). Thirty-five percent wash out. Twenty percent survive to graduation. The ones who graduate are the ones who can calculate a charge size while being shot at, place it accurately while explosions happen around them, and then walk away from the detonation without looking back.*

---

### Chapter II: Of Training

Heavy Engineer training lasts **eight months**.

**Phase One — Physical Conditioning (2 months):** Candidates learn to move in heavy armor. They run, climb, crawl, carry. The armor never comes off. Candidates sleep in it, eat in it, live in it.

**Phase Two — Explosives Theory (2 months):** Chemistry, physics, application. Calculating charge sizes, predicting blast effects, understanding material reactions. They also study accidents and visit the sites where previous candidates died.

**Phase Three — Gauntlet Training (2 months):** Candidates learn to use their gauntlets — the sparking, crackling weapons for close combat. Fire, shock, acid, force. A well-placed punch can be as effective as a grenade, and much more personal.

**Phase Four — Field Integration (2 months):** Assigned to active units as apprentices, working under senior Heavy Engineers. Those who survive this phase graduate. Those who do not are buried with full honors, and their names are added to the wall of fallen engineers.

> *The wall of fallen Heavy Engineers is covered with names, but also with small drawings of explosions, added by those who remember. No one knows who started this tradition. No one knows why it continues. But every name has an explosion next to it, drawn in charcoal or soot or, in some cases, burned directly into the stone.*

## PART THE SEVENTH: OF THE LIFE

### The Daily Routine

A Heavy Engineer's day begins before dawn, in the cold and the dark, with the sound of artillery in the distance. They check their armor — every plate, every seal, every joint. They check their gauntlets — power cells, focusing crystals, elemental chambers. They check their grenades — every one, carefully, counting and recounting.

They eat what they can. They do not taste the food. They are thinking about the explosions to come.

They join their unit and move to the line. The soldiers step aside as they pass. They maintain a respectful distance. They know what the engineer carries.

The battle begins. The engineer works.

When the battle ends, they check their equipment again. The armor. The gauntlets. The grenades. Everything must be perfect for the next battle.

They sleep in their armor, usually. They wake when the next battle begins.

### The Relationship With Soldiers

Soldiers have a complicated relationship with their Heavy Engineer. They are **grateful** — the engineer's explosions clear the way, break the enemy, save lives. They are **afraid** — one stray spark and the whole unit could disappear. They **respect** the engineer — toughest person on the battlefield and everyone knows it. They maintain their distance. Not out of dislike. Out of survival. The engineer understands.

### The Relationship With the Grenades

Engineers know their grenades individually. This one has a slightly shorter fuse. This one has a crack in the casing. This one is the one they'll use when nothing else works. They know them like old friends.

Engineers talk to their grenades. "Not yet," they might say, when an enemy gets too close. "Soon," when the perfect moment approaches. "Goodbye," when they throw the last one.

### The Cost

- **Physical:** Weight of armor, strain on joints, cumulative effect of countless explosions. Engineers age faster. Their hearing fades. Their hands shake.
- **Emotional:** Responsibility, pressure, the knowledge that one mistake can kill everyone nearby.
- **Social:** Distance, isolation, the knowledge that even your allies are afraid of you.
- **The cost of the explosions themselves.** Every grenade thrown is a grenade gone. Eventually, there is nothing left to give.

---

## PART THE EIGHTH: OF THE LEGACY

### The Obstacles Removed

The primary legacy of a Heavy Engineer is the obstacles they removed. Every wall that fell, every bunker that collapsed, every enemy that was erased from existence — they are the engineer's legacy.

A single engineer, in a single battle, can remove obstacles that would stop an entire army. Over a career, dozens of battles. The ripple effects of those removals extend for years. Most engineers never see these ripples. They do their work and move on.

### The Craters Left Behind

Heavy Engineers also leave behind craters. Every explosion creates one — a mark on the landscape, a scar on the earth, a reminder that something happened here.

These craters become landmarks. "The big crater" becomes a reference point. "The triple crater" becomes a warning. "The crater with the engineer's name" becomes a memorial. Some craters last for generations.

### The Stories

The story of the engineer who breached the wall single-handed. The story of the engineer who held the line with nothing but grenades and guts. The story of the engineer who stood at the center of the explosion and walked away.

These stories are told and retold, passed down through generations. The monks collect them. They have hundreds, recorded on paper and parchment and, in some cases, carved into the walls of the monastery. They read them when they need to remember why the world is worth watching.

---

## EPILOGUE: ON THE WEIGHT OF OBSTACLES REMOVED

The monks asked an old engineer once — a woman who had served forty years, who had removed more obstacles than anyone could count — what she remembered most.

She thought about it for a long time. The monks waited. They are good at waiting.

*"I remember the explosions,"* she said finally. *"Every one of them. The sound. The light. The way the ground shook. The way the enemy disappeared."*

She paused.

*"I remember the craters, too. All of them. I used to visit them, sometimes, after the battles. Just to see what I'd done. To remind myself."*

She paused again.

*"I remember the soldiers who stood beside me. Some of them are gone now. But I remember them."*

The monks asked if she regretted it. If she would do it again.

She laughed. It was a loud laugh, the laugh of someone who has spent too long near explosions.

*"Every day,"* she said. *"And I would. Every day. Because the obstacles need to be removed. Someone has to remove them. Might as well be me."*

# THE BOOK OF THE IRONCLAD

## PART THE FIRST: OF THE IRONCLAD IN GENERAL

### Chapter I: Of the Nature of the Profession

The Ironclad occupies a unique position in the military hierarchy of Great Terra. They are not soldiers, though they fight. They are not engineers, though they understand machines. They are not tanks, though they function as such. They are something else entirely: living fortresses, walking bunkers, people who have decided that the best way to survive combat is to be too tough to kill.

The Ironclad's philosophy can be summarized in six words: **raw strength, hydraulic pressure, break defenses.** They do not dodge. They do not take cover. They do not retreat. They advance, slowly and inevitably, and everything in their path is either destroyed or pushed aside. They are the embodiment of industrial might — pistons, steam, grinding metal, and the absolute certainty that they will reach their objective because nothing can stop them.

The aesthetic is pure industrial plating — thick armor, hydraulic pistons, heavy boots that leave dents in the ground. Steam vents release pressure with each step. Metal grinds against metal. The Ironclad is not a person wearing armor. They are a machine with a person inside, and sometimes it's hard to tell where one ends and the other begins.

> *The monks have observed that Ironclads tend to develop a particular relationship with momentum. They understand that once something heavy starts moving, it is very difficult to stop. They apply this principle to themselves. They start moving. They keep moving. They do not stop until the enemy is gone.*
>
> *The monks have also observed that Ironclads are remarkably popular among the troops they fight alongside, though at an even greater distance than Heavy Engineers. This is not because they are unpleasant people — many are quite pleasant, in a slow, deliberate way. It is because being near an Ironclad means being near something that is about to hit something else very hard, and the area around that impact is not safe. Soldiers have learned to stay behind the Ironclad. This is not a metaphor.*

---

### Chapter II: Of the Origins

The Ironclad as a distinct profession emerged from the heavy construction and demolition industries of early Terra. Before the war, before the Hive, before any of it, there were people who operated the largest machines — cranes that lifted tons, drills that broke mountains, hammers that could level buildings.

When the war began, these operators were conscripted like everyone else. But they brought something with them: an understanding of hydraulic force, of momentum, of what happens when immense weight meets immovable object. They knew that the right application of force could break anything.

The first Ironclads were simply construction workers in reinforced armor, carrying industrial tools into battle. They wore the heaviest plates available, carried hammers meant for breaking rock, and advanced on the enemy with the same steady pace they'd used to approach a demolition site. It worked surprisingly well.

Two hundred and fifty years later, the Ironclad has evolved. The armor is better. The hydraulics are more sophisticated. The training is more formal. But the core remains the same: take someone who understands force, point them at the enemy, and watch the enemy's lines crumble.

> *The Aetherian Republic has nothing like the Ironclad. They consider such brute force tactics crude, inelegant, a violation of the principles of precision warfare. This has not stopped them from developing their own heavy assault units, of course — they just call them something else and pretend they're for defensive purposes only. The Ironclads find this hilarious, usually while something behind them collapses.*

---

### Chapter III: Of the Philosophy

The Ironclad's philosophy is built on a simple observation: **everything breaks eventually.**

Stone breaks. Steel breaks. Bodies break. Fortifications break. Armies break. Everything, given enough force applied at the right point, will eventually cease to be a problem.

The Ironclad does not need to be fast. Speed is for people who are afraid of being hit. The Ironclad is not afraid of being hit. The armor will absorb it. The hydraulics will compensate. The body will endure.

The Ironclad does not need to be clever. Cleverness is for people who cannot rely on force. The Ironclad needs only to be steady. To advance. To apply pressure. To keep moving forward until the enemy runs out of things to throw and places to hide.

## PART THE SECOND: OF THE BECOMING

### Chapter I: Of Selection

**Initial Screening:** Candidates are identified during basic training or recruited from heavy industrial backgrounds. They must demonstrate three qualities: physical size (the armor is heavy), mental fortitude (the work is boring), and a certain disregard for personal safety (the ability to stand still while being shot).

**The Aptitude Test:**
- *Physical:* Carry increasingly heavy loads over increasingly difficult terrain while wearing increasingly heavy armor. Those who fail are returned to their units.
- *Pain Tolerance:* Subjected to controlled impacts — first light, then heavy, then very heavy. Must learn to absorb the hits, to keep their feet, to keep advancing.
- *Pressure:* Placed in a simulated combat situation and required to advance under fire. Cannot take cover. Cannot retreat. Must simply walk forward, absorbing hits, until they reach their objective.

**The Final Interview:** The few candidates who survive meet with a senior Ironclad, who explains the long hours in heavy armor, the constant pressure of being the target, the knowledge that you will be hit, many times, and you must keep going. Most candidates withdraw. The ones who stay become Ironclads.

> *The attrition rate for Ironclad training is approximately 80%. Twenty percent die during training (usually from impacts). Forty percent wash out. Twenty percent survive to graduation. The ones who graduate are the ones who can walk through anything, absorb anything, and keep going. They are, quite literally, the toughest people in the Terran military.*

---

### Chapter II: Of Training

Ironclad training lasts **one year**.

**Phase One — Physical Conditioning (4 months):** Candidates learn to move in heavy armor. Walk, run (slowly), climb (carefully), carry. The armor never comes off. They also learn to absorb impacts — padded weapons, then real weapons, then things that are not weapons at all.

**Phase Two — Hydraulic Systems (3 months):** Everything about the armor's hydraulic systems — pressure, maintenance, repair. Also the Pneumatic Hammer: swing it, control it, deliver maximum force with minimum effort.

**Phase Three — Shield Work (3 months):** The Riot Shield — block, deflect, absorb. Shield Bash timing, Fortress Stance, the art of being the target, of drawing fire, of protecting those behind them.

**Phase Four — Field Integration (2 months):** Assigned to active units as apprentices. Those who survive graduate. Those who do not are buried with full honors, and their names are added to the wall of fallen Ironclads.

> *The wall of fallen Ironclads is the largest of all, and the most dented. Every name has a hammer shape next to it, etched into the stone with the same care that the Ironclad put into every swing.*

## PART THE SEVENTH: OF THE LIFE

### The Daily Routine

An Ironclad's day begins before dawn. They check their armor — every plate, every seal, every hydraulic line. They check their hammer. They check their shield. They eat what they can and do not taste the food.

They join their unit and move to the line. The soldiers nod as they pass and step aside. They know what the Ironclad does. They know that as long as the Ironclad is in front, they are safe.

The battle begins. The Ironclad advances.

They absorb hits that would kill anyone else. They swing the hammer into enemy lines. When the battle ends, the armor is dented, the hammer is stained, and the shield is scarred. They will repair everything tonight. Tomorrow they advance again.

### The Relationship With Soldiers

Soldiers **trust** the Ironclad — who stands in front, takes the hits, makes survival possible. They **respect** the Ironclad — toughest, strongest, most determined. They **stay behind** the Ironclad — not out of fear, out of tactics. The Ironclad is the shield. They are the sword. Together, they win.

And when the Ironclad falls, they mourn. Then they find a new Ironclad and advance again.

### The Relationship With the Armor

Ironclads know their armor intimately — every dent, every scratch, every repair, which plates are strongest, which hydraulics are fastest. They talk to it during long nights. They personify it. When the armor holds, it's stubborn. When it fails, it's betrayal. When it needs repair, it's asking for attention.

When a suit is damaged beyond repair, the Ironclad feels it. They salvage what they can. They build new armor from the remains. It's not the same. But it's close.

### The Cost

- **Physical:** Weight of armor, grinding joints, aching backs, bodies covered in scars that never quite heal. Ironclads age faster than other soldiers.
- **Emotional:** The responsibility of being what everyone stands behind. The weight is invisible, but it is heavy.
- **Social:** Isolation, separation, being different. They live in a world of armor and hydraulics.
- **The cost of the hits themselves.** Every bullet absorbed is a bullet that could have killed. Each one leaves a mark, even if only on the armor.

## PART THE EIGHTH: OF THE LEGACY

### The Lines Held

The primary legacy of an Ironclad is the lines they held. Every position that did not fall, every advance that was stopped, every enemy that was turned back — they are the Ironclad's legacy.

A single Ironclad, in a single battle, can hold a line that would otherwise break. Over a career, dozens. The ripple effects of those holds extend for years. Most Ironclads never see these ripples. They do their work and move on.

### The Dents Left Behind

Ironclads leave behind dents. In their armor. In the ground. In the enemy. Every impact creates a mark.

These dents become stories. The dent from the shell that almost killed them. The dent from the hammer that broke the wall. Some dents are passed down with the armor, added to with each battle, until the armor is more dent than plate. The Ironclad who wore it may be forgotten. But the dents remain.

### The Stories

The story of the Ironclad who held the bridge alone. The story of the Ironclad who walked through artillery to reach the enemy. The story of the Ironclad who fell still standing, hammer raised, facing the enemy until the end.

The monks collect these stories. They are always loud. They are stories of impact, of force, of lines held.

## EPILOGUE: ON THE WEIGHT OF LINES HELD

The monks asked an old Ironclad once — a woman who had served forty years, who had held more lines than anyone could count — what she remembered most.

She thought about it for a long time. The monks waited. They are good at waiting.

*"I remember the hits,"* she said finally. *"Every one of them. The bullets. The shells. The blades. The way they felt against the armor. The way the hydraulics compensated. The way I kept walking."*

She paused.

*"I remember the hammer swings, too. The way it felt when it connected. The way the enemy flew backward. The way the ground shook."*

She paused again.

*"I remember the soldiers behind me. The ones I protected. Some of them are gone now. But I remember them."*

The monks asked if she regretted it. If she would do it again.

She laughed. It was a deep laugh, the laugh of someone who has spent too long in heavy armor.

*"Every day,"* she said. *"And I would. Every day. Because the line needs to be held. Someone has to hold it. Might as well be me."*

# THE BOOK OF THE FIELD MEDIC

## PART THE FIRST: OF THE FIELD MEDIC IN GENERAL

### Chapter I: Of the Nature of the Profession

The Field Medic occupies a peculiar position in the military hierarchy of both Great Terra and the Aetherian Republic. They are not quite soldiers, though they carry weapons and wear uniforms. They are not quite doctors, though they perform medicine that would make a civilian physician weep. They are something in between — a hybrid creature bred by the peculiar demands of industrial warfare.

The Field Medic's philosophy can be summarized in six words: **restoration, stitching, and chemical stabilization.** They do not have the luxury of sterile operating theaters. They do not have the time for careful diagnosis. They work in the mud, under fire, with whatever supplies they can carry.

The aesthetic is gritty dieselpunk — everything made of metal, running on steam or petroleum, designed by someone who had never heard the word "ergonomics" but was very good at "will this survive being dropped from a moving vehicle?"

> *The monks have observed that Field Medics tend to develop a particular sense of humor — dark, pragmatic, and deeply inappropriate for polite company. When you spend your days patching up soldiers who have been shot, stabbed, burned, poisoned, and occasionally exploded, you either develop a sense of humor or you develop a drinking problem. Many develop both.*
>
> *The monks have also observed that Field Medics are remarkably popular among the troops. This is not because of their sparkling personalities. It is because they are the ones who keep you from bleeding to death. Gratitude is a powerful bonding agent. Soldiers have been known to take bullets intended for their medic — not out of selfless heroism, but out of the cold calculation that a dead medic cannot patch anyone up.*

---

### Chapter II: Of the Origins

The Field Medic as a distinct profession emerged during the early years of the Skyward Schism, when both sides realized that soldiers were dying not from the severity of their wounds but from the time it took to get them to a surgeon.

In the first battles, wounded soldiers lay where they fell, sometimes for hours, sometimes for days. The lucky ones died quickly. The unlucky ones bled out slowly, or died of shock, or were taken by the Abominations that came to feed when the fighting stopped.

The commanders on both sides recognized that this was unacceptable — not for humanitarian reasons, but because trained soldiers were expensive to replace. A wounded veteran was worth more than a raw recruit.

The solution was the Field Medic: a soldier trained in basic medicine, equipped with portable supplies, deployed directly to the front. If they died in the process — well, medics were cheaper to train than soldiers. This calculus has not changed in two hundred and fifty years.

> *The first Field Medics were volunteers from the medical corps who believed they could make a difference. Most of them died in the first year. They were replaced by conscripts from the regular army who had shown some aptitude for not panicking under fire. The quality of care declined, but the quantity of medics increased. This is the story of war in miniature: ideals replaced by expediency, quality by quantity, hope by endurance.*

---

### Chapter III: Of the Three Traditions

**The Terran Tradition:** Pragmatists. Industrial-grade supplies, crude but effective tools, healing that is aggressive — hit hard, hit fast, let the patient recover later. The body is a machine; wounds are malfunctions; healing is repair. Use whatever works without worrying too much about side effects or long-term consequences. The goal is to get the soldier back to the fight.

**The Aetherian Tradition:** Precisionists. Refined crystal compounds, elegant tools, healing that works with the body rather than against it — steady, predictable, often beautiful. The body is a harmony; wounds are disruptions; healing is restoration. Work carefully, precisely, using refined compounds and tuned crystals to encourage the body to heal itself. The process matters as much as the outcome.

**The Discarded Adaptation:** No formal tradition. When someone gets hurt, whoever is closest and knows something steps forward. Terran bandages, Aetherian crystals, Abomination parts, anything that might help. The body is a mystery; wounds are opportunities for learning; healing is whatever works. A Discarded healer will try anything once.

> *The monks interviewed medics from all three traditions. The Terran medics talked about equipment and technique. The Aetherian medics talked about philosophy and approach. The Discarded medics told stories — long, rambling, frequently hilarious stories about the time they saved someone with a piece of wire and a prayer, or the time they accidentally poisoned a patient and had to save them again. The monks learned more from the stories than from all the technical explanations combined.*

---

## PART THE SECOND: OF THE BECOMING

### Chapter I: Of Selection

**Terran Selection:** Recruits who show steady hands, quick thinking, and the ability to remain calm under fire are offered a choice: continue as a line soldier (life expectancy measured in months) or train as a medic (also months, but more interesting months). Most accept. The alternative is the trenches.

Tests: medical knowledge (can you identify major organs?), manual dexterity (can you stitch a wound without fainting?), psychological resilience (can you watch a man die without breaking down?).

**Aetherian Selection:** Candidates identified early, sometimes in childhood. Years of study — anatomy, chemistry, crystal resonance, philosophical foundations — before they ever see a battlefield. Far better trained than Terran counterparts, far less experienced in actual combat. The Aetherians consider this acceptable. The Terrans consider it ridiculous.

**Discarded Selection:** No selection process. When someone gets hurt, whoever is closest tries to help. If they succeed, they become the medic. If they fail, someone else tries next time.

> *The monks have observed that the best medics often come from the worst circumstances. Knowledge is useful. Experience is essential. Desperation is sometimes the most useful of all.*

---

### Chapter II: Of Training

**Terran Training — The Six-Week Miracle:** Six weeks, because the trenches need medics *now*. Basic anatomy, wound assessment, field suturing, chemical administration, triage. At the end, issued a basic kit and sent to the front. Most will be dead within three months. Those who survive will have learned more in those three months than in all their training.

**Aetherian Training — The Years of Preparation:** Five to seven years. Comprehensive anatomy, advanced chemistry, crystal resonance, surgical technique, medical philosophy — the ethics of healing, the meaning of health, the purpose of medicine in a just society. Their survival rate is higher than their Terran counterparts, but not by as much as you might think. The front does not care how many years you studied.

**Discarded Training — Learning by Doing:** Someone gets hurt. You try to help. If they live, you learned something. If they die, you learned something else. They also learn from each other, sharing techniques in an oral tradition spanning generations — fragmentary, inconsistent, sometimes contradictory, but deep, practical, and grounded in centuries of survival.

> *The monks asked Gristle — an old woman who had been keeping people alive in the Wastes for fifty years — how she learned her craft. She laughed. "Trial and error. Mostly error. The trick is to make sure your errors don't kill anyone. Or if they do, make sure you learn something before you try again. Also, don't make the same error twice. That's just embarrassing."*

## PART THE SEVENTH: OF THE LIFE

### The Daily Routine

A Field Medic's day begins before dawn. Check equipment — syringes, canisters, bandages, stimulants. Eat what they can; they have stopped tasting food. Join the unit. Move to the line. The soldiers nod. Some smile. The ones who do not smile are the ones who have been wounded before. They know what's coming.

The battle begins. The medic works. They stitch wounds, inject stimulants, apply bandages, make decisions about who lives and who dies. They do not think about these decisions. Thinking takes time, and time is the one thing they do not have.

When the battle ends, they collapse. They sleep where they fall. They wake when the next battle begins.

### The Patients

Some are grateful. Some curse the medic for saving them, because saving them means sending them back to the fight. The gratitude and the anger are both the same thing: fear, expressing itself in different ways. The medic learns not to take it personally.

The medic learns to work quickly, efficiently, without getting attached. A patient you get attached to is a patient whose death will destroy you.

### The Ones Who Cannot Be Saved

Every medic has patients they cannot save. The first one is the hardest — that face, forever. The hundredth one is easier. Not easy. Easier. The thousandth one is just another patient. The medic does not remember their face.

Some medics stop counting. Some medics never start. Some medics count every one, carrying the numbers like weights, hoping that one day the counting will mean something. It never does.

### The Relationship With Other Medics

Field Medics have a special bond. When two medics meet, they exchange stories — techniques that work, techniques that don't, patients saved, patients lost. They do not judge each other. They know that judgment is easy when you weren't there.

On the battlefield, a medic who sees another medic in trouble will drop everything to help. This is not heroism. It is practicality. A dead medic helps no one. A live medic helps everyone.

In rare moments of peace, medics gather. They drink. They talk. They laugh at jokes that would make anyone else sick. They are the only ones who understand.

## EPILOGUE: ON THE WEIGHT OF LIVES SAVED

The monks asked a retired Field Medic once — a woman who had served thirty years in the trenches of Windward Pass, who had saved hundreds of lives and lost count of how many — what she remembered most.

She thought about it for a long time. The monks waited. They are good at waiting.

*"I remember the ones I couldn't save,"* she said finally. *"Not all of them. Just... some of them. The ones who looked at me while they died. The ones who asked me to tell their families something. The ones who were so young they shouldn't have been there at all."*

She paused.

*"I remember the ones I saved, too. But that's different. The ones I saved, they went on to live their lives. They became someone else's memory. The ones I lost — they're still with me. They're always with me."*

The monks asked if she regretted it. If she would do it again.

She laughed. It was not a happy laugh.

*"Every day,"* she said. *"And I would. Every day."*

The monks have thought about this conversation many times since. The weight of lives saved is not a burden. It is a purpose. Field Medics carry that purpose with them, through every battle, through every death, through every impossible choice. They carry it because someone has to.

# THE BOOK OF THE IRON APOTHECARY

*Being a Complete Account of the Industrial Disruptors Who Bring Systemic Failure to the Enemies of Great Terra, as Compiled by the Chroniclers of the Iron Monastery From Interviews with Surviving Apothecaries, the Hazardous Remains of Those Who Were Not So Fortunate, and the Carefully Filtered Air of Their Workshops*

## Part the First: Of the Iron Apothecary in General

### Chapter I: Of the Nature of the Profession

The Iron Apothecary occupies a unique position in the military hierarchy of Great Terra. They are not healers. They are not engineers. They are industrial chemists who have turned their knowledge of toxins, corrosives, and systemic failure into weapons of war.

**Philosophy:** Systemic failure. They do not seek to kill quickly. They seek to dismantle the enemy from within — introduce compounds that cause the body to fail, armor to corrode, the mind to cloud. They are patient. They understand that death delivered slowly is death that spreads fear.

**Aesthetic:** Piecraftian dieselpunk — gritty, dark, worn. Gas mask worn at all times. Gear covered in green stains, chemical burns. They smell faintly of industrial solvent and something that might be described as regret, if regret had a smell.

The monks have observed that Iron Apothecaries develop a particular detachment from normal human concerns. They watch enemies die slowly without flinching. They handle compounds that would kill a normal person with casual familiarity.

*Footnote: Iron Apothecaries are remarkably unpopular among the troops they fight alongside. Soldiers have learned to stand upwind.*

### Chapter II: Of Origins

The Iron Apothecary program emerged from the chemical weapons programs of the early war. The first apothecaries were volunteers from the chemical industry — factory workers, laboratory technicians, engineers. Given military training, chemical weapons training, and a simple mission: find new ways to make the enemy die. Two hundred and fifty years later, the program is still running.

*Footnote: The Aetherian Republic considers chemical warfare barbaric. This has not stopped them from developing their own chemical weapons. They just call them something else. The apothecaries find this hilarious.*

### Chapter III: Of the Philosophy

Everything fails eventually. Metal corrodes. Flesh decays. Minds cloud. Organs shut down. Given enough time and the right conditions, every system breaks down. The apothecary's job is to provide the conditions.

This is not cruelty, they would tell you. It is efficiency. A bullet kills one person. A well-designed toxin can kill a dozen, and the survivors will spend days wondering if they're next.

The apothecary does not hate the enemy. Hatred is inefficient. They simply understand that the enemy is a system, and systems can be made to fail.

## Part the Second: Of the Becoming

### Chapter I: Of Selection

**Initial Screening:** Intelligence, steady nerves, and detachment from normal human concerns.

**The Aptitude Test:** Chemical knowledge, manual dexterity under pressure (many candidates die), and psychological resilience (ability to watch suffering without emotion).

**The Final Interview:** A senior apothecary explains what the job actually entails. Most candidates withdraw. The ones who stay become apothecaries.

*Footnote: Attrition rate approximately 80%. Twenty percent die during training. Forty percent wash out. Twenty percent graduate.*

### Chapter II: Of Training

- Phase One: Basic Chemistry (3 months) — organic, inorganic, industrial compounds
- Phase Two: Advanced Toxinology (3 months) — neurotoxins, hemotoxins, cytotoxins; also antidotes

- Phase Four: Field Integration (3 months) — apprenticeship under senior apothecaries

1. **Priority target?** → Hemotoxin Impale (if ready)
2. **Enemy cluster?** → Corrosive Grenade
3. **Armored enemies?** → Acid Stream (ignore armor) + Corrosive Payload
4. **Crowd control needed?** → Choking Gas (stun/blind threat)
5. **Ally debuffed or wounded?** → Antitoxin Jab / Combat Stim
6. **About to die?** → Hazmat Lockdown
7. **Nothing else?** → Impale with best payload, or Syringe Lunge to build poison stacks

## Epilogue: On the Weight of Deaths Caused

*An old apothecary, forty years of service, hundreds of kills:*

"I remember the first one. Not his face — he was wearing a mask. But I remember the way he fell. The way his body convulsed. The way he stopped moving.

I remember the last one, too. Yesterday. Some soldier, young, probably conscripted. He looked at me before he died. Through the gas. Through the filters. He looked at me, and I looked at him, and then he died.

The ones in between — I don't remember them. There are too many. They blur together. They become just numbers. Just statistics. Just enemies.

The work matters. That's what I tell myself. The work matters. The killing matters. It keeps our people alive. That's enough. That has to be enough."

# THE BOOK OF TRUTHS

*Being a Philosophical Reflection on the Nature of Existence, the Meaning of Conflict, and the Strange Persistence of Hope in a World That Seems Determined to Destroy Itself*

## Part the First: Of the Nature of Conflict

### Why We Fight

**Fear drives each faction differently:**
- **Terra** fears running out — mines dying, resources dwindling, the machine demands fuel
- **Aetheria** fears dying — corruption spreading, crystals dimming, isolation the only protection
- **The Discarded** fear being forgotten — all that remains are stories
- **The Deep Iron** fear the dark — not physical darkness, but what stirs in the deepest places
- **The Skyborn** fear being tied down — freedom as survival
- **Machine Communion** fears the flesh — weakness, brevity, unpredictability
- **The Hollow** fear nothing. That is perhaps the most frightening thing of all.

**Pride sustains what fear starts.** Terra cannot imagine alternatives to dominance. Aetheria cannot see its own flaws. The Discarded are resistant to change. The Deep Iron too patient to act. The Skyborn uncommitted. The Communion arrogant in evolution.

**Trauma repeats itself.** Terran children hear Aetherian atrocity stories. Aetherian children hear Terran barbarism stories. Neither entirely false. Neither entirely true. Both believed. Belief is enough. The cycle continues generation after generation because no one knows how to stop it.

**And yet — hope persists.** The Armistice of Silence exists. Every night in the Trenches, enemies become allies. They share fire and rations and stories. They learn each other's names. It cannot be killed, no matter how hard the world tries.

## Part the Second: Of the Nature of Power

### The Six Tragic Strengths

**Terra's power is industrial.** Control of resources = control of war. But it requires constant consumption. The mines must keep producing. They cannot stop. This drives Project Lazarus — an attempt to create power from death itself. **Tragedy: to know you are destroying yourself, and be unable to stop.**

**Aetheria's power is intellectual.** They cannot match Terra's production, but can outthink them. But knowledge requires isolation to remain pure. Walls also imprison. **Tragedy: to know you are dying, and be unable to reach out for help.**

**The Discarded's power is adaptive.** They became something new, something that survives where old bodies could not. But adaptation requires forgetting. Grimka "The Unbroken" has killed more Abominations than anyone. She cannot remember why she started. **Tragedy: to be so focused on survival that you forget what you're surviving for.**

**Deep Iron power is patient.** They see disaster coming, see the same mistakes being repeated. They could warn, could act. They do not. They wait. Patience becomes paralysis. **Tragedy: to see disaster coming, and be unable to do anything.**

**Skyborn power is commercial.** No territory to defend, no ideology to protect. Resilient through movement. But rootlessness becomes emptiness. **Tragedy: to be free, and to be lonely.**

**Communion power is transformative.** They offer immortality, strength, perfection. The price is everything you were. Sister Chroma remembers flashes of her former life — a face, a name, a feeling. They fade. **Tragedy: to gain everything, and to lose yourself.**

**Hollow power is terrible.** They remember being human. They remember warmth, love, hope. They remember losing it all. The Watcher has stood in Aetherion's central plaza for millennia. It does not intervene. It waits. For what? No one knows. Not even the Watcher. **Tragedy: to remember what you were, and know you can never be it again.**

## Part the Third: Of Small Graces

The Armistice of Silence is grace — two hundred and fifty years of war, and still every night enemies become allies.

The stories the Discarded preserve are grace — the refusal to let the past die.

The sunrise over the Gray Wastes is grace — another day. Another chance.

The cheese at the Iron Monastery is grace. It is made by monks who have dedicated their lives to the craft. You may exchange a good story for a wheel. A great story is worth a lifetime supply.

## Part the Fourth: Of the Monks' Purpose

The monks watch because watching is what they are qualified to do. Every civilization that has fallen thought it was special. Thought it would last forever. The monks do not presume to know better. They watch. They wait. They write things down.

They hope that someday, someone will learn.

*Beneath the humility, beneath the patience, beneath the purpose, there is hope. They do not act on this hope. They cannot. Action is not their role. But they feel it, quietly, in the quiet moments between watching.*

## Epilogue

The monks asked an old trader — fifty years in the Floating Markets — what the most valuable thing she had ever handled was.

"A story," she said. "A soldier came to me once. He had nothing — no Scraps, no Shards. Just a story about something he'd seen in the Gray Wastes. Something that shouldn't exist.

I traded him a week's rations for that story. Best trade I ever made. I still tell it. People pay to hear it.

That's the thing about value. It's not about metal or crystals. It's about what people need. What people want. What people will remember when everything else is gone."

*Footnote from the Chief Chronicler: The cheese, as always, is excellent. Perhaps that is the answer. Perhaps the point is not to find meaning, but to create it. To make something good, something lasting, something that brings joy to others. To make cheese. To share it. To be grateful. Perhaps that is everything.*

# ON THE MATTER OF CURRENCY AND EXCHANGE

*Being a Thorough Account of What Passes for Money in a World Gone Mad*

## Primary Currencies

### Iron Scraps (Great Terra)

**Nature:** Exactly what they sound like — scraps of iron, steel, machine parts, industrial waste repurposed as money. Emerged organically as Terra produced vast quantities of metal waste.

**Icon:** Rusty gear, metal fragment, remnant of machinery. Design varies by region. Some officially stamped; most are not.

**Value Reference:**

| Amount | Purchasing Power |
|--------|-----------------|
| 1 Scrap | A basic bolt or washer. Minimum unit. |
| 10 Scraps | Dirty bandage, emergency rations, a few rounds of ammunition |
| 50 Scraps | Low-tier weapon — rusty but functional |
| 100 Scraps | Medium-tier weapon or armor piece |
| 500 Scraps | High-tier equipment, best mass-produced gear |

**Cultural significance:** Terrans believe in utility. A thing's value is determined by what it can do. Scraps can always be melted down and reforged. They are never worthless.

*Footnote: Prices in the Hive vary significantly. The same bolt that costs 1 Scrap in the Lower Rigs might cost 5 Scraps in the Spires — "artisanal" and "hand-crafted."*

### Aetherium Shards (Aetherian Republic)

**Nature:** Fragments of refined Aetherium crystal, deliberately manufactured to precise sizes and shapes. Glow with soft blue light — intensity varies with purity and size.

**Icon:** Glowing blue crystal fragment. Smaller shards rough-cut; larger ones faceted. Most valuable shards are works of art.

**Value Reference:**

| Amount | Purchasing Power |
|--------|-----------------|
| 1 Shard | Minimal energy charge; light for a night |
| 5 Shards | Aetherian mountain-grown coffee (excellent) or a meal |
| 25 Shards | Low-tier tech weapon |
| 50 Shards | Medium-tier precision weapon or armor |
| 250 Shards | High-tier equipment — weapons that sing, armor that shifts |

**Cultural significance:** Aetherians believe in beauty. Their currency glows, is cut precisely, is a pleasure to hold. Purity — the refinement process removes impurities, produces something clean and clear.

*Note: Flickering shards are unstable and should be exchanged immediately.*

## Exchange Rates

**Official Rate (government fiction):** 1 Shard = 2 Scraps  
**Gray Market Rate (reality):** 1 Shard = 3–4 Scraps, fluctuating

Gray market rate favors Shards because: more versatile (currency + energy + healing component + crafting), harder to counterfeit (requires crystal refinement), and beautiful (people pay extra for beautiful things).
